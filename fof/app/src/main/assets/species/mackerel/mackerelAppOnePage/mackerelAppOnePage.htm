The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   publishes a   European   Red   List   providing   a   review   of   the   conservation status   of   European   species   identifying   those   species   that   are threatened    with    extinction    at    the    regional    level,    so    that appropriate    conservation    action    can    be    taken    to    improve their status. The Mackerel conservation status is  ‘Least Concern’
The    Inshore    Fishery    and    Conservation    Authorities    (IFCA) have   considerable   responsibility   for   managing   fish   stocks   out to   6   miles   from   the   shore.   While   the   IFCA's   still   have   to adhere    to    the    rules    laid    down    by    the    European    Union Common   Fisheries   Policy   they   are   able,   through   bylaws,   to set   minimum   landing   sizes   that   exceed   the   European   Union's and    apply    to    all    fish    retained    including    those    caught    by recreational anglers.

MACKEREL (

Scomber scombrus)

These   fishes   are   found   in   topical   and   warm   temperate   seas around     the     world,     with     relatively     few     species     moving seasonally    into    cool    temperate    seas,    such    as    those    of northern Europe. They   are   predatory   and   live   in   the   surface   waters   of   the   sea in sometimes huge schools. It   is   found   seasonally   close   inshore   as   well   as   offshore   banks but   highly   migratory   making   inshore   migrations   as   well   as moving   north   in   the   summer.   The   reverse   movement   occurs as the seas cool in winter.
Additional    help    to    identify    this    species    can    be    found    in Google images using the following link; www.google.com
Back   is   brilliant   blue-green   with   black   irregular   curving   lines, which are sometimes broken with spots and curves. The lower sides   and   belly   are   white   with   pinkish   and   gold   reflections. The   blues   and   iridescent   colours   on   the   sides   fade   quickly after death.
Mackerel   are   caught   mainly   on   feathers   which   are   worked   by sending   them   down   to   the   seabed   then   jigging   while   reeling up. Do not let the line go slack as the tackle will get tangled. When   there   is   an   onshore   wind,   Mackerel   are   offshore   and vice   versa,   but   prolonged   rough   seas   break   up   the   schools   to make fishing patchy. When   the   fish   is   hooked,   leave   the   feathers   at   the   same depth   for   a   short   while   as   the   vigorous   shaking   so   caused will    attract    others.    Once    caught,    keep    Mackerel    cool    and away from sunlight as they quickly deteriorate.
Attains   a   maximum   length   of   66   cms   and   a   weight   of   3.3   kg rarely   more   than   1.8   kg.   The   usual   length   today   is   40.6   cms and weight of 0.6 kg.
A    large    family    named    Scombridae    that    include    Spanish mackerel,   Frigate   mackerel   and   Mackerel   amongst   numerous Tunnies.
The   Atlantic   mackerel   was   first   described   in   1758   by   Swedish zoologist    Carl    Linnaeus    in    his    10th    edition    of    Systema Naturae.    Linnaeus    gave    it    the    scientific    name    'Scomber scomber'   from   the   Greek   word   'Skombros'   meaning   'Tunny'   or 'Mackerel'. It   is   an   active,   fast-moving   fish   that   must   keep   in   constant motion   to   bring   in   enough   oxygen   to   survive.   They   form   large schools,   consisting   of   individuals   of   the   same   relative   size, near the ocean surface during all seasons but winter. During   winter   it   stays   on   the   edge   of   the   continental   shelf where   it   lies   quietly   as   there   is   a   scarcity   of   food   and   needs to   partially   fast.   It   swims   using   short   movements   of   the   rear of its body and the caudal fin. At   all   seasons   of   the   year   they   are   preyed   upon   by   larger fishes.    To    survive    natural    predators,    Mackerel    use    their ability   to   dive   deep   and   surface   without   harm   as   they   have   no swim   bladder.   Also,   this   fish   can   retract   its   dorsal   fins   into grooves    in    the    body    to    streamline    its    shape    for    fast swimming.    Unlike    other    mackerel,    Atlantic    Mackerel    don't leap out of the water unless attempting to escape a predator. Mackerel    are    slow-growing,    taking    three    to    four-year    post larvae to reach maturity. When   feeding   on   larger   prey,   schools   tend   to   break   down   into shoals     and     individuals     find     food     on     their     own.     When consuming   plankton,   however,   they   form   tight   aggregations, open    their    mouths    as    wide    as    possible,    and    extend    their operculums,   swimming   in   a   tightly-packed   school   that   acts like   a   series   of   miniature   tow   nets.   Spaced   only   about   the diameter   of   a   single   fish's   mouth   apart,   this   formation   greatly reduces   the   ability   of   plankton   to   evade   capture,   as   plankton darting   out   of   the   way   of   one   fish   is   likely   to   end   up   in   the jaws of another. A   highly   commercial   species,   the   Atlantic   mackerel   is   sought after   for   its   red   meat,   which   is   strong   in   flavour   and   high   in oil   content   and   Omega-3   fatty   acids   among   other   nutrients containing   nearly   twice   as   much   per   unit   weight   as   salmon. Unlike    the    King    and    Spanish    species,    Northern    Atlantic mackerel   are   very   low   in   mercury,   and   can   be   eaten   at   least twice a week. Nearly   1   million   tonnes   of   Atlantic   mackerel   are   caught   each year   globally,   the   bulk   of   which   is   sold   fresh,   frozen,   smoked, or   canned.   Despite   its   highly   commercial   status,   the   Atlantic mackerel    is    listed    as    'Least    Concern'    by    the    International Union   for   the   Conservation   of   Nature,   meaning   this   species   is abundant   and   wide   spread,   and   global   catch   has   remained sustainable.   However,   the   IFCA   has   restrictions   in   place   for Eastern   and   Kent   and   Essex   regions.   These   restrictions   could range    from    a    total    ban    to    limited    day    catch    or    restricted zones.    Always    check    beforehand    as    fines    for    landing    are substantial.

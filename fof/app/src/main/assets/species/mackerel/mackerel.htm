The   Atlantic   mackerel   was   first   described   in   1758   by   Swedish   zoologist Carl   Linnaeus   in   his   10th   edition   of   Systema   Naturae.   Linnaeus   gave   it the     scientific     name     'Scomber     scomber'     from     the     Greek     word 'Skombros' meaning 'Tunny' or 'Mackerel'. It   is   an   active,   fast-moving   fish   that   must   keep   in   constant   motion   to bring   in   enough   oxygen   to   survive.   They   form   large   schools,   consisting of   individuals   of   the   same   relative   size,   near   the   ocean   surface   during all   seasons   but   winter.   During   winter   it   stays   on   the   edge   of   the continental   shelf   where   it   lies   quietly   as   there   is   a   scarcity   of   food   and needs   to   partially   fast.   It   swims   using   short   movements   of   the   rear   of its body and the caudal fin. At   all   seasons   of   the   year   they   are   preyed   upon   by   larger   fishes.   To survive   natural   predators,   Mackerel   use   their   ability   to   dive   deep   and surface   without   harm   as   they   have   no   swim   bladder.   Also,   this   fish   can retract   its   dorsal   fins   into   grooves   in   the   body   to   streamline   its   shape for   fast   swimming.   Unlike   other   mackerel,   Atlantic   Mackerel   don't   leap out of the water unless attempting to escape a predator. Mackerel   are   slow-growing,   taking   three   to   four-year   post   larvae   to reach maturity. When   feeding   on   larger   prey,   schools   tend   to   break   down   into   shoals and   individuals   find   food   on   their   own.   When   consuming   plankton, however,   they   form   tight   aggregations,   open   their   mouths   as   wide   as possible,   and   extend   their   operculums,   swimming   in   a   tightly-packed school   that   acts   like   a   series   of   miniature   tow   nets.   Spaced   only   about the   diameter   of   a   single   fish's   mouth   apart,   this   formation   greatly reduces   the   ability   of   plankton   to   evade   capture,   as   plankton   darting out of the way of one fish is likely to end up in the jaws of another. A   highly   commercial   species,   the   Atlantic   mackerel   is   sought   after   for its   red   meat,   which   is   strong   in   flavour   and   high   in   oil   content   and Omega-3   fatty   acids   among   other   nutrients   containing   nearly   twice   as much   per   unit   weight   as   salmon.   Unlike   the   King   and   Spanish   species, Northern   Atlantic   mackerel   are   very   low   in   mercury,   and   can   be   eaten at least twice a week. Nearly   1   million   tonnes   of   Atlantic   mackerel   are   caught   each   year globally,   the   bulk   of   which   is   sold   fresh,   frozen,   smoked,   or   canned. Despite   its   highly   commercial   status,   the   Atlantic   mackerel   is   listed   as 'Least   Concern'   by   the   International   Union   for   the   Conservation   of Nature,   meaning   this   species   is   abundant   and   wide   spread,   and   global catch has remained sustainable. However,   the   IFCA   has   restrictions   in   place   for   Eastern   and   Kent   and Essex   regions.   These   restrictions   could   range   from   a   total   ban   to limited   day   catch   or   restricted   zones.   Always   check   beforehand   as   fines for landing are substantial. Mackerel   are   caught   mainly   on   feathers   which   are   worked   by   sending them   down   to   the   seabed   then   jigging   while   reeling   up.   Do   not   let   the line go slack as the tackle will get tangled. When   there   is   an   onshore   wind,   Mackerel   are   offshore   and   vice   versa, but prolonged rough seas break up the schools to make fishing patchy. When   the   fish   is   hooked,   leave   the   feathers   at   the   same   depth   for   a short   while   as   the   vigorous   shaking   so   caused   will   attract   others.   Once caught,   keep   Mackerel   cool   and   away   from   sunlight   as   they   quickly deteriorate.

The   Saithe   has   little   to   differentiate   it   from   the   Pollack   which   is   a   near relative.   The   simplest   way   to   recognise   them   is   to   check   whether   the lower   jaw   does   not   protrude,   or   the   lateral   line   is   almost   straight,   and the   tail   fin   is   forked.   They   are   in   general   are   larger   than   Pollack, especially the mature fish in deep water. A   schooling   fish   which   matures   between   five   and   ten   years,   the   female sheds    pelagic    eggs    during    January    to    April    at    depths    of    100-200 metres in water of high salinity. The   eggs/larvae   drift   near   the   surface   and   are   carried   to   shallower nursery   areas   where   they   can   be   found   amongst   rocks   and   sea   weed until they reach the age of three years. Saithe   young   are   also   frequently   seen   in   northern   harbours,   but   the mature fish prefer deep water around wrecks and reefs. The   species   migrates   extensively   either   at   great   depths   or   near   the surface, usually following herring or sprat shoals. The   uncooked   flesh   is   darkly   coloured,   hence   the   alternative   name Coalfish,   which   may   have   led   to   the   undeserved   reputation   of   this   fish as poor for eating. Saithe   is   edible   and   has   commercial   value,   and   sold   fresh,   frozen, smoked   or   dried   and   salted   although   it   is   considerably   less   valuable than    premium    whitefish    such    as    Cod    and    Haddock.    To    achieve    a 'salmon-like'   orange   colour,   it   can   be   salted   and   smoked.   In   Germany, the    fish    is    commonly    sold    as    'Seelachs'    (literally    'sea    salmon'), although it is not closely related to any salmon. In   sporting   terms,   the   fish   ranks   with   the   best,   but   like   the   Pollack   and Pout   suffer   from   an   expanding   swim   bladder   when   brought   up   too quickly.   A   slow   retrieval   rate   increases   the   possibility   of   keeping   the fish alive. As   this   fish   can   make   powerful   lunges   and   runs,   keep   the   reel   clutch slack   so   any   sudden   surge   does   not   break   the   line   -   however,   do   not give too much away so that it can get back among the rocks or wreck.

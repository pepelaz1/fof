SAITHE

 (Pollachius virens) 

aka: Billet, Coalfish, Coley

Additional   help   to   identify   this   species   can   be   found   in   Google images using this location;   www.google.com
Very   dark   green   on   the   back   and   upper   sides,   sharply   giving way   to   dull   silvery   sides   and   belly.   The   lateral   line   is   creamy coloured.
In   sporting   terms,   the   fish   ranks   with   the   best,   but   like   the Pollack   and   Pout   suffer   from   an   expanding   swim   bladder   when brought    up    too    quickly.    A    slow    retrieval    rate    increases    the possibility of keeping the fish alive. As   this   fish   can   make   powerful   lunges   and   runs,   keep   the   reel clutch   fairly   slack   so   any   sudden   surge   does   not   break   the   line   - however,   do   not   give   too   much   away   so   that   it   can   get   back among the rocks or wreck.

FACTS

The    members    of    the    cod    family    (Gadidae)    are,    with    one exception,    all    marine    fishes.    Most    are    found    in    the    cool temperate   waters   of   the   northern   hemisphere   generally   on   or close to the continental shelf. Few   are   found   in   the   deep   sea   and   then   only   near   the   surface or   in   the   southern   hemisphere.   Their   fins   lack   spines,   and they   usually   have   2   or   3   dorsal   and   1   or   2   anal   fins.   A   barbel on   the   chin   is   normal,   and   they   all   have   small   to   minute scales. Approximately    100    species    are    known    world-wide,    but    no more   than   30   live   in   European   waters   one   of   which   is   the Saithe.
The   Saithe   has   little   to   differentiate   it   from   the   Pollack   which is   a   near   relative.   The   simplest   way   to   recognise   them   is   to check   whether   the   lower   jaw   does   not   protrude,   or   the   lateral line   is   almost   straight,   and   the   tail   fin   is   forked.   They   are   in general   are   larger   than   Pollack,   especially   the   mature   fish   in deep water. A   schooling   fish   which   matures   between   five   and   ten   years, the    female    sheds    pelagic    eggs    during    January    to    April    at depths of 100-200 metres in water of high salinity. The    eggs/larvae    drift    near    the    surface    and    are    carried    to shallower   nursery   areas   where   they   can   be   found   amongst rocks and sea weed until they reach the age of three years. Saithe   young   are   also   frequently   seen   in   northern   harbours, but   the   mature   fish   prefer   deep   water   around   wrecks   and reefs. The   species   migrates   extensively   either   at   great   depths   or near the surface, usually following herring or sprat shoals. The   uncooked   flesh   is   darkly   coloured,   hence   the   alternative name    Coalfish,    which    may    have    led    to    the    undeserved reputation of this fish as poor for eating. Coalfish   is   edible   and   has   commercial   value,   and   sold   fresh, frozen,   smoked   or   dried   and   salted   although   it   is   considerably less    valuable    than    premium    whitefish    such    as    Cod    and Haddock.   To   achieve   a   'salmon-like'   orange   colour,   it   can   be salted   and   smoked.   In   Germany,   the   fish   is   commonly   sold   as 'Seelachs'   (literally   'sea   salmon'),   although   it   is   not   closely related to any salmon.

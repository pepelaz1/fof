BREAM, Black (

Spondyliosoma cantharus)

It   is   particularly   common   around   wrecks   and   rocky   outcrops and   migrates   from   southern   waters   in   April   /   June   to   spawn along   chalk   outcrops   off   the   south   and   south   west   coast   of England.
Additional   help   to   identify   this   species   can   be   found   in   Google images using the following link; www.google.com
Greyish    on    the    back    sides    silvery    grey    with    6    or    7    dusky vertical   bars.   The   fins   are   grey   or   light   brown   the   edge   of   the tail fin dusky.
Ensure   that   you   are   entitled   to   catch   this   species   as   there are many restrictions on numbers and sites. Active   mainly   during   the   day,   the   best   time   to   fish   for   Bream is   just   before   and   after   slack   water   as   they   prefer   to   feed during   weak   tidal   runs.   Once   hooked,   the   Bream   will   give excellent   sport   on   light   tackle,   especially   if   using   fresh   water spinning rod. Always   use   a   net   to   land   your   catch   as   it   may   only   be   lip hooked.   For   bait   use   long   strips   of   Squid   or   Mackerel   (with   or without    a    worm    cocktail)    which    are    most    effective    when presented above the lead weight.
Exceptionally   it   attains   a   length   of   51   cms;   mostly   it   is   about 35 cms and a weight of around 3 kg.
Classed   as   'Dermersal',   living   close   to   or   on   the   seabed   and part   of   the   Sparidae   family   of   marine   fishes   bream   are   found around   the   world   in   tropical   or   temperate   seas.   There   are many   other   fishes   related   to   Black   Bream   e.g.   Red-sea   Bream (Pagellus   bogaraveo),   Pandora   (Pagelluserythrinus),   Spanish sea-bream     (Pagellus     acarne)     etc     amongst     others.     Eight species   have   been   reported   in   northern   European   waters   but only two are at all common.
A   relatively   common   sea-bream   in   northern   European   waters, although    north    of    the    English    Channel    it    is    probably    a summertime   migrant.   The   Black   Bream   is   not   really   black   but has   silvery   grey   sides   with   dusky   bars.   These   colours   fade soon after death when they turn almost black. Black   sea   bream   are   protogynous   meaning   females   have   the ability to change to males. Juveniles    are    more    colourful    and    usually    have    numerous broken   yellowish   stripes   along   the   sides,   spotted   fins   and   a dark edge to the tail which often persists into adult life. It   is   one   of   the   few   sea   species   in   which   the   male   builds   a nest   by   digging   a   depression   in   the   sandy   sea-bed   in   which the   female   lays   her   eggs   in   April   and   May.   Here   they   are guarded   by   the   male   until   they   hatch;   the   young   tend   to   form a    loose    school    around    the    nest    for    several    weeks    after hatching. To   ensure   that   you   do   not   get   any   injuries   hold   the   belly when   disgorging   and   beware   of   sharp   spines   on   the   dorsal and anal fins.
*   There   are   restrictions   in   places,   sizes   and   times   you   should check local by-laws before targeting this species.

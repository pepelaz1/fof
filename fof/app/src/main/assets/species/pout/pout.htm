The   Pout   is   another   member   of   the   Cod   family   but   the   least   attractive for   eating.   An   extremely   common   schooling   fish,   it   lives   up   to   three   or four years. Pouting   are   scavengers   which   feed   on   the   seabed.   They   forage   for   any food   source   they   can   find   with   marine   worms,   shellfish   and   dead   fish all   making   up   their   diet.   Due   to   their   small   size   pouting   is   a   source   of prey for large species such as cod, bass and conger eels. It   is   good   for   making   up   catch   numbers   in   competitions   and   very   small Pout   can   be   put   aside   as   live   bait   for   bigger   fish.   Otherwise   this   fish   is not   worth   keeping   as   the   flesh   is   soft,   bony   and   spoils   quickly;   on   the other   hand,   crabbers   like   them   for   their   pots,   so   they   are   good   for something! Pout   does   not   often   survive   being   brought   to   the   surface   from   deep water   as   the   swim   bladder   expands   with   the   change   of   water   pressure. When   Pout   suddenly   stop   feeding   it   is   a   sign   that   larger   predatory   fish are in the area. The   Pout   will   take   any   bait   on   any   rig   anytime,   especially   at   night   and it   is   generally   considered   a   nuisance   by   causing   havoc   with   baits   for other species, especially when night fishing for Conger. For   good   sport   inshore   drifting   or   trolling   using   feathers   and   spinning at   dawn   and   dusk   or   even   on   dull   summer   days   produces   the   juvenile fish that live in shallow waters over rocks. Large   solitary   Pout   in   deep   water   are   taken   by   using   amongst   other rigs   the   Three   Hook   Paternoster   rig.   During   tide   runs   this   method   will yield   good-sized   specimens,   especially   in   mid-water   above   wrecks   or reefs. It   helps   to   keep   the   bait   on   the   move   by   lowering   to   the   bottom   and slowly   retrieving.   Lengthen   the   trace   as   the   tide   slackens   so   that   there is plenty of movement of the bait. On   bringing   the   Pout   up   from   the   depths   the   swim   bladder   expands,   so do   this   slowly   to   avoid   impairing   its   resistance   or,   indeed,   killing   the fish which if possible can be returned to the sea.

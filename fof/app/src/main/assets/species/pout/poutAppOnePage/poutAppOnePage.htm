A   very   common   fish   in   inshore   waters,   particularly   in   rocky areas    where    large    schools    form    above    reefs    and    wrecks. Small   Pout   are   also   very   abundant   over   in   shallow   water   over sand.   Larger   Pout   (over   0.5   kilos)   are   caught   only   in   deep water around wrecks and reefs.

The    members    of    the    cod    family    (Gadidae)    are,    with    one exception,    all    marine    fishes.    Most    are    found    in    the    cool temperate   waters   of   the   northern   hemisphere   generally   on   or close to the continental shelf. Few   are   found   in   the   deep   sea   and   then   only   near   the   surface or   in   the   southern   hemisphere.   Their   fins   lack   spines,   and they   usually   have   2   or   3   dorsal   and   1   or   2   anal   fins.   A   barbel on   the   chin   is   normal,   and   they   all   have   small   to   minute scales. Approximately    100    species    are    known    world-wide,    but    no more    than30    live    in    European    waters    one    of    which    is    the Pout.

FACTS

The   Pout   is   another   member   of   the   Cod   family   but   the   least attractive   for   eating.   An   extremely   common   schooling   fish, it lives up to three or four years. Pouting    are    scavengers    which    feed    on    the    seabed.    They forage    for    any    food    source    they    can    find    with    marine worms, shellfish and dead fish all making up their diet. Due   to   their   small   size   pouting   is   a   source   of   prey   for   large species such as cod, bass and conger eels. It   is   good   for   making   up   catch   numbers   in   competitions   and very   small   Pout   can   be   put   aside   as   live   bait   for   bigger   fish. Otherwise   this   fish   is   not   worth   keeping   as   the   flesh   is   soft, bony   and   spoils   quickly;   on   the   other   hand,   crabbers   like them for their pots, so they are good for something! When   Pout   suddenly   stop   feeding   it   is   a   sign   that   larger predatory fish are in the area.

The   Pout   will   take   any   bait   on   any   rig   anytime,   especially   at   night and   it   is   generally   considered   a   nuisance   by   causing   havoc   with baits for other species, especially when night fishing for Conger. For   good   sport   inshore   drift   feathering,   trolling   or   spinning   at   dawn and   dusk   or   on   dull   summer   days   produces   the   juvenile   fish   that live in shallow waters over rocks. Large   solitary   Pout   in   deep   water   are   taken   by   using   a   Three   Hook Paternoster   rig.   During   tide   runs   this   method   will   yield   good-sized specimens, especially in mid-water above wrecks or reefs. Keep   the   bait   on   the   move   by   lowering   to   the   bottom   and   slowly retrieving.   Lengthen   the   trace   as   the   tide   slackens   so   that   there   is plenty of movement of the bait. On    bringing    the    Pout    up    from    the    depths    the    swim    bladder expands,   so   do   this   slowly   to   avoid   impairing   its   resistance   or, indeed, killing the fish which if possible is returned to the sea.

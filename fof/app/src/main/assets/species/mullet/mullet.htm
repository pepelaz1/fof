The   Thick   lipped   Mullet   species   is   the   largest   and   most   common   of   the Grey   mullets,   a   family   which   include   the   Thin   Lipped   and   Golden   Grey. The Grey is the only one of the three that is found in Irish waters. The   Thin   Lipped   is   relatively   rare,   with   an   average   weight   of   around 2.7kg   and   has   a   black   spot   at   the   base   of   the   pectoral   fin.   The   Golden Grey is similar but has a golden blotch on the gill cover. A   common   inhabitant   of   coastal   waters   of   all   Europe   except   most northerly   parts.   To   some   extent   it   is   migratory   arriving   inshore   as   the sea    temperature    rises    in    May/June    northwards    with    summertime warming    of    the    sea    and    moving    back    southwards    in    summertime before   winter   when   shoals   have   been   spotted   swimming   near   the surface   in   their   thousands   It   is   particularly   common   close   inshore   in harbour   mouths,   estuaries,   in   sandy   bays,   and   in   the   channels   of Saltings;   in   calm   water   it   can   be   seen   cruising   in   schools   at   the   surface with its snout almost breaking through an on occasions doing so. Having   a   thick-walled   gizzard   like   stomach   and   a   very   long   intestine adapted   to   the   extract   the   best   out   of   the   rich   organic   material   of surface   mud   and   algae   which   is   gulped   in   mouthfuls,   rejecting   the greater   part   of   the   indigestible   mud   through   gills   and   swallowing   the plant   matter,   nematode   worms,   copepods,   and   other   animals   with   a good   mixture   of   sand.   Large   Grey   Mullet   also   feed   on   molluscs   and small crustaceans. This   species   is   of   some   value   commercially   and   is   also   good   sport fishing even if hard to hook and capture. Although   not   on   the   IUCN   ‘Red   List’   there   are   restrictions   by   the regional   IFCAs   in   particular   for   the   North   West   and   Southern   regions for    minimum    landing    sizes.    Always    check    beforehand    as    fines    for landing are substantial. Because   it   is   a   very   wary   feeder,   catching   this   fish   provides   a   serious challenge   if   you   are   unable   to   get   to   sea   due   to   the   weather.   Try around harbour walls, moorings and river estuaries. As   the   diet   consists   mainly   of   soft   foodstuffs   (weed,   worms   and   algae from   the   sea   bed   or   underwater   structures)   if   possible   groundbait   the area   for   a   few   days   prior   to   fishing   it,   little   and   often   is   said   to   be   the rule. Without prior groundbait a small bait feeder is sometimes used. Hooks    are    likely    to    tear    out,    so    play    very    carefully    using    gentle pressure   and   giving   line   when   necessary   on   strong   runs.   A   landing   net is   essential   and   keep   hooks   and   trace   line   very   light   for   optimum results. You   are   very   unlikely   to   want   to   eat   Mullet   as   it   tastes   muddy,   so   best return your catch to the sea unharmed.

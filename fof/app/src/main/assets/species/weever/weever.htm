The   Weever   is   included   not   to   encourage   you   to   catch   it   but   to   enable you   to   recognise   this   fish   and   be   warned   of   the   dangers   should   you hook one. The   name   Weever   is   believed   to   derive   from   the   Old   French   word, "Wivier"    meaning    viper.    The    Lesser    Weever    is    smaller    and    more common   than   the   Greater   Weever   but   both   have   venom   glands   on their first dorsal fin and have spines on each gill cover. The   Lesser   Weever   is   littoral,   (extends   from   the   high-water   mark, which   is   rarely   inundated,   to   shoreline   areas   that   are   permanently submerged) and benthic (area of continental shelf). During    the    daytime,    resting    on    the    bottom,    its    position    can    be described   as   with   eyes   buried   and   the   tip   of   the   first   dorsal   fin,   where the   venom   glands   are   located,   is   exposed.   The   exposed   black   dorsal   fin may   warn   off   predatory   fish.   It   has   been   suggested   that   the   Weever's lustrous   and   mobile   eyes   serve   to   lure   its   prey   closer   while   its   jaws, set    at    a    very    acute    angle,    allow    it    to    snap    up    small    fish    and crustaceans, even when half buried. The   venom   is   similar   to   that   of   certain   snakes   in   that   it   affects   both   the nervous   system   and   the   blood   of   the   victim.   The   sting   is   acute   and intense. The   pain   frequently   is   radiated   to   the   area   around   the   limb.   The seriousness   of   the   pain   reaches   its   peak   30   minutes   after   the   sting, and   then   slowly   decreases.   However,   some   pain   (or   other   sensation, such   as   a   tingle)   may   continue   to   affect   the   area   for   up   to   24   hours. Very   rarely,   pain   can   be   propagated   to   the   tributary   lymph   nodes,   i.e. those   in   the   groin   (when   the   sting   is   on   the   sole   of   the   foot),   or   those in the armpit (if the sting is on the hands). The   symptoms   are   fainting,   palpitations,   fever,   delirium,   vomiting   and, in extreme cases, heart failure. As   there   is   no   specific   antidote   for   any   fish   venom,   the   immediate recommended   treatment   to   destroy   the   venom's   toxicity.   The   best   first aid   is   to   wash   the   wound   and   to   ease   the   pain   soak   the   infected   area in   water   as   hot,   40°C   (104°F)   or   as   hot   as   the   patient   can   bear   for   up to   one   hour   as   soon   as   possible   after   the   injury.   At   the   same   time make   the   patient   as   comfortable   as   possible   and   summon   medical assistance.   Also,   reassure   the   patient   of   the   relative   harmlessness   of the sting. Because   of   its   venom   and   its   occurrence   near   beaches,   it   is   one   of Europe's   most   dangerous   Weever   species   and   has   the   most   potent toxin of all the Weevers. The   Common   Sole   which   has   deep   a   black   patch   on   its   upper   pectoral fm   may   sometimes   mimic   the   Weever   by   raising   this   fin   like   a   small flag and hence it is left alone! The   Weever   is   virtually   inedible   and   if   caught   should   only   be   handled with   extreme   care.   To   kill,   stab   in   the   back   of   the   head,   cut   the   trace, sacrificing   the   hook,   and   dispose   of   the   fish   carefully   as   the   venomous spines remain toxic long after death.

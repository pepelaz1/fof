Spurdog,   which   used   to   be   numbering   thousands   (in   waters   of   20-400 metres),    hunt    other    schooling    fish    close    to    the    sea    bed    when    in shallow   water   and   in   mid-water   elsewhere,   rising   to   the   surface   at night.   As   migratory   fish,   they   are   only   found   in   runs   as   they   pass through   an   area.   They   prefer   a   water   temperature   between   6   and 15°C. Males   are   identified   by   a   pair   of   pelvic   fins   modified   as   sperm-transfer organs,    or    "claspers".    Reproduction    is    aplacental    viviparous    where fertilization   is   internal.   The   male   inserts   one   clasper   into   the   female oviduct   orifice   and   injects   sperm   along   a   groove   on   the   claspers' dorsal    section.    Immediately    following    fertilization,    the    eggs    are surrounded   by   thin   shells   called   "candles"   with   one   candle   usually surrounding   several   eggs.   Mating   takes   place   in   the   winter   months with   gestation   lasting   22–24   months.   Litters   range   between   2   and   11 but average 6 or 7. The   species,   which   live   20   to   26   years,   grow   to   nearly   1m   in   20   years. By   this   time   the   Spurdog   will   have   attained   its   maximum   weight   of   10 kg   and   its   age   can   be   measured   by   counting   the   ridges   on   the   spine   of the second dorsal fin. The   spines   on   the   front   of   the   dorsal   are   highly   developed   dermal teeth    connected    to    poison    glands    and    used    as    weapons    of    self defence.   These   spines   can   inflict   a   very   painful   wound,   and   in   the   past were   snapped   off   with   pliers   unless   they   were   return   to   the   sea,   as they are part of its defence system. Owing   to   a   long   gestation   period   and   the   rather   large   size   at   which maturity is reached, the Spurdog is liable to suffer from overfishing. Once   considered   a   nuisance   or   pest   due   to   damaging   nets   and   line caught   fish   it   was   commercially   caught   for   the   liver   to   provide   oil,   fish meal and for human consumption. Spiny   dogfish   are   fished   for   food   in   Europe   where   the   meat   is   primarily consumed   and   in   the   UK   was   sold   in   fish   and   chip   shops   as   "Huss", and    it    was    historically    sold    as    "rock    salmon"    until    this    term    was outlawed   by   consumer   legislation.   It   is   also   being   called   'Flake'.   The fins   and   tails   are   processed   for   less   expensive   versions   of   shark   fin soup in Chinese cuisine. Once   the   most   abundant   shark   species   in   the   world,   populations   of Squalus   acanthias   have   declined   significantly.   They   are   classified   in   the IUCN   'Red   List   of   threatened   species'   as   'Vulnerable'   globally   and 'Critically    endangered'    in    the    Northeast    Atlantic,    meaning    stocks around   Europe   have   decreased   by   at   least   95%.   This   is   a   direct   result of overfishing to supply northern Europe. In   EU   waters,   a   Total   Allowable   Catch   (TAC)   for   2011   was   set   at   zero tons,    ending    targeted    fishing    for    the    species.    This    remains    even though it is reported that populations have recovered. In   recent   years   medical   research   has   discovered   that   body   tissues   of the    shark    family    produce    powerful    antibodies    which    have    been extracted to manufacture antibiotics. Due   to   it’s   vulnerable   status   and   even   though   there   are   no   restrictions on    non-commercial    catch    the    recommendation    is    to    return    any Spurdog to help stocks recover.

The    Hake    possibly    gets    its    name    from    the    German    word    'lecht', meaning   Pike   which   it   slightly   resembles   and   although   once   included   in the Cod family, Hake are now classed separately. The   Hake   largely   inhabits   sub-tropical   regions,   in   the   middle   and   lower continental   shelf,   though   different   species   are   to   be   found   in   sub-Arctic and Antarctic waters. The   female   spawnâ€™s   pelagic   eggs   in   well   defined   areas   at   around   200   m in   spring   of   summer   (the   later   further   north).   The   eggs   and   larvae   are pelagic   and   tend   to   drift   into   inshore   waters   where   the   young   are found during their first year of life. The   young   feed   on   crustaceans,   but   as   they   grow,   they   start   to   feed   on small    and    medium-sized    fish    and    cephalopods.    The    adults    show cannibalistic behaviour, eating smaller members of their own species. In    times    past    this    fish    was    important    commercially    being    caught mainly   in   trawls,   but   because   it   is   slow-growing   (females   don't   mature until   their   tenth   year)   stocks   are   depleted   and   it   is   now   relatively scarce, even to anglers and large species are very uncommon. Normally   resting   near   the   sea   bed   during   the   day,   the   fish   rises   at night   to   hunt   in   mid-water.   Summer   is   the   only   season   to   fish   for   Hake, using deep water tackle and rigs at night. If   it   is   too   deep   to   anchor,   drifting   and   using   pirks   sent   to   the   bottom and jigged on the retrieve is recommended. As   Hake   have   very   sharp   teeth   a   length   of   wire   trace   to   the   hook   is essential   and   a   snap-swivel   above   the   end   tackle   will   save   getting fingers too close to those teeth when changing traces in the dark.

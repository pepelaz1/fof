HAKE (

Merluccius merluccius)

Hake   are   widely   distributed   inhabit   the   sea   bed   on   the   middle and   continental   shelf   at   depths   of   150   -   550   m   coming   closer inshore,   mainly   on   the   western   Atlantic   coasts   and   around Scotland   during   summer.   It   lives   near   the   bottom   rather   than on it and makes feeding forays into mid-water at night.
Additional    help    to    identify    this    species    can    be    found    in Google images using the following link;   www.google.com
Blue-grey    on    the    back,    silvery    on    the    sides    and    silvery white ventrally. Inside the mouth and gill cavity black.
Attains    1.80    m    in    length    but    is    rarely    more    than    1    m.    A weight   of   11.7   kg   has   been   recorded   but   5   kg   is   probably near the average maximum..
The   Hakes   are   a   group   of   cod-like   fishes   which   were   included in   the   cod   family   but   are   now   regarded   as   distinct   enough   to be accorded family status as the Merlucciidae.
The    Hake    possibly    gets    its    name    from    the    German    word 'lecht',   meaning   Pike   which   it   slightly   resembles   and   although once    included    in    the    Cod    family,    Hake    are    now    classed separately. The   Hake   largely   inhabits   sub-tropical   regions,   in   the   middle and   lower   continental   shelf,   though   different   species   are   to   be found in sub-Arctic and Antarctic waters. The    female    spawn’s    pelagic    eggs    in    well    defined    areas    at around   200   m   in   spring   of   summer   (the   later   further   north). The   eggs   and   larvae   are   pelagic   and   tend   to   drift   into   inshore waters   where   the   young   are   found   during   their   first   year   of life. The   young   feed   on   crustaceans,   but   as   they   grow,   they   start to   feed   on   small   and   medium-sized   fish   and   cephalopods.   The adults   show   cannibalistic   behaviour,   eating   smaller   members of their own species. In    times    past    this    fish    was    important    commercially    being caught    mainly    in    trawls,    but    because    it    is    slow-growing (females    don't    mature    until    their    tenth    year)    stocks    are depleted   and   it   is   now   relatively   scarce,   even   to   anglers   and large species are very uncommon.

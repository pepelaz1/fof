The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   publishes a   European   Red   List   providing   a   review   of   the   conservation status   of   European   species   identifying   those   species   that   are threatened    with    extinction    at    the    regional    level,    so    that appropriate    conservation    action    can    be    taken    to    improve their status. The Halibut conservation status is  ‘Vulnerable’.
The    Inshore    Fishery    and    Conservation    Authorities    (IFCA) have   considerable   responsibility   for   managing   fish   stocks   out to   6   miles   from   the   shore.   While   the   IFCA's   still   have   to adhere    to    the    rules    laid    down    by    the    European    Union Common   Fisheries   Policy   they   are   able,   through   bylaws,   to set   minimum   landing   sizes   that   exceed   the   European   Union's and    apply    to    all    fish    retained    including    those    caught    by recreational anglers.
British Record Fish Committee The    Committee    recognises    and    publish    record    weights    of both   fresh   and   salt   water   fish   caught   on   rod   and   line   by   fair angling methods in the waters of the UK. Latest records can be found at:   www.anglingtrust.net

HALIBUT (

Hippoglosus hippoglosus)

Halibut   are   found   living   in   a   wide   range   of   sea-beds,   from sand,    gravel    and    rocky    grounds,    also    at    different    depths depending   on   between   50   and   2,000   m   their   age.   They   are not   confined   to   the   seabed,   as   are   most   flatfishes,   for   it   is   an active predator which forages mid water.
Additional    help    to    identify    this    species    can    be    found    in Google images using the following link;   www.google.com
Dark   greeny-brown,   sometimes   almost   black;   the   blind   side is pearly white.
Attains      a   maximum   length   of   2.54   m   and   a   weight   of   320   kg has   been   recorded   but   today   rarely   above   2   m.   Records   of Halibut   of   3   -   4   m   exist   from   the   period   before   the   stocks were heavily fished'
A   large   family   (Pleuronectidae)   of   flatfishes   which   all   have both   eyes   on   the   right   side   of   the   body,   the   left   eye   moving over    the    head    during    metamorphosis    from    post    larva    to bottom   living   young.   Reversed   examples,   those   which   have both   eyes   on   the   left   side   are   very   common   in   some   species but relatively rare with Dab. Eight   species   are   known   from   northern   European   seas;   Dab, Plaice,    Flounder,    Lemon    sole,    Witch,    Long    Rough    Dab    and Halibut
The   Halibut   is   among   the   largest   teleost   (ray   finned),   bony, flatfish,   fish   in   the   world,   and   is   an   endangered   species   due to   a   slow   rate   of   growth   and   previous   overfishing.   Halibut   are strong   swimmers   and   can   migrate   long   distances.   Halibut   size is   not   age-specific,   but   rather   tends   to   follow   a   cycle   related to halibut (and therefore food) abundance. Females   are   larger   than   males   at   all   ages   and   outlive   them   to 35   to   50   years   growing   to   around   three   metres   in   length   with a   weight   of   180   kilos.   Preferring   cold   water   in   the   summer months,   this   species   is   found   in   the   eastern   Atlantic   around Ireland   and   Scotland   as   well   as   further   round   to   Orkney   and Shetland. The   fish   gets   its   name   from   'Hall',   meaning   holy   and   'Butt',   a name    for    Flounder,    as    it    comes    from    this    family    and    was eaten    on    holy    days.    A    very    palatable    and    commercially important   fish,   the   larger   specimens   are   now   becoming   rare through over fishing. An   active   predator,   its   jaws   and   teeth   are   equally   developed on   both   sides   to   grab   free-swimming   prey   as   it   rises   from   the sea   bed   to   feed   on   other   fish.   On   the   reverse   Halibut   are eaten by seals and is a staple food of the Greenland shark. Spawning   in   winter   to   early   spring   close   to   the   seabed   in depths   of   400   m   to   1000   m   on   the   edge   of   the   continental shelf.   The   female,   quicker-growing   and   larger   than   the   male, sheds    around    2    million    pelagic    eggs,    which    after    hatching move   down   to   the   sea   bed   and   develop   on   their   right   side. Spawning    areas    have    been    protected    to    conserve    stocks. After   spawning   they,   with   the   males,   migrate   into   the   richer offshore feeding grounds. The   young   fish   live   close   inshore   for   between   2   to   4   years then   gradually   move   to   deeper   water   until   they   are   sexually mature at 10 to 14 years. Due    to    its    popularity    as    a    food    fish,    Atlantic    halibut    has attracted     investment     in     fish     farming.     As     of     2006     five countries:   Canada,   Norway,   UK,   Iceland   and   Chile   have   been engaged in some form of aquaculture production. The   American   Fisheries   Society   has   classified   the   species   as "Vulnerable".   In   2010,   Greenpeace   International   added   the Atlantic    Halibut    to    its    seafood    red    list    of    fish    that    are commonly   sold   in   supermarkets   around   the   world,   and   which have   a   very   high   risk   of   being   sourced   from   unsustainable fisheries. The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   (IUCN) has   added   this   species   to   their   ‘RedList’   as   ‘Vulnerable’,   a high     risk     of     becoming     endangered,     which     results     in restrictions for targeting and landing this species.

The   Halibut   is   among   the   largest   teleost   (ray   finned),      bony,   flatfish, fish   in   the   world,   and   is   an   endangered   species   due   to   a   slow   rate   of growth   and   previous   overfishing.   Halibut   are   strong   swimmers   and   are able   to   migrate   long   distances.   Halibut   size   is   not   age-specific,   but rather   tends   to   follow   a   cycle   related   to   halibut   (and   therefore   food) abundance. Females   are   larger   than   males   at   all   ages   and   outlive   them   to   35   to   50 years   growing   to   around   three   metres   in   length   with   a   weight   of   180 kilos.   Preferring   cold   water   in   the   summer   months,   this   species   is found   in   the   eastern   Atlantic   around   Ireland   and   Scotland   as   well   as further round to Orkney and Shetland. The   fish   gets   its   name   from   'Hall',   meaning   holy   and   'Butt',   a   name   for Flounder,   as   it   comes   from   this   family   and   was   eaten   on   holy   days.   A very   palatable   and   commercially   important   fish,   the   larger   specimens are now becoming rare through over fishing. An   active   predator,   its   jaws   and   teeth   are   equally   developed   on   both sides   to   grab   free-swimming   prey   as   it   rises   from   the   sea   bed   to   feed on   other   fish.   On   the   reverse   Halibut   are   eaten   by   seals   and   is   a   staple food of the Greenland shark. Spawning   in   winter   to   early   spring   close   to   the   seabed   in   depths   of   400 m   to   1000   m   on   the   edge   of   the   continental   shelf.   The   female,   quicker- growing   and   larger   than   the   male,   sheds   around   2   million   pelagic   eggs, which   after   hatching   move   down   to   the   sea   bed   and   develop   on   their right   side.   Spawning   areas   have   been   protected   to   conserve   stocks. After   spawning   they,   with   the   males,   migrate   into   the   richer   offshore feeding grounds. The    young    fish    live    close    inshore    for    between    2    to    4    years    then gradually   move   to   deeper   water   until   they   are   sexually   mature   at   10   to 14 years. Due   to   its   popularity   as   a   food   fish,   Atlantic   halibut   has   attracted investment   in   fish   farming.   As   of   2006   five   countries:   Canada,   Norway, UK,   Iceland   and   Chile   have   been   engaged   in   some   form   of   aquaculture production. The     American     Fisheries     Society     has     classified     the     species     as "Vulnerable".   In   2010,   Greenpeace   International   added   the   Atlantic Halibut    to    its    seafood    red    list    of    fish    that    are    commonly    sold    in supermarkets   around   the   world,   and   which   have   a   very   high   risk   of being sourced from unsustainable fisheries.

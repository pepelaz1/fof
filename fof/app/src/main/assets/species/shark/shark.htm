The    etymology    of    the    word    "Porbeagle"    is    obscure.    A    common suggestion   is   that   it   combines   "porpoise"   and   "beagle",   referencing this shark's shape and tenacious hunting habits. Porbeagle   are   fast   and   highly   active,   with   physiological   adaptations that    enable    it    to    maintain    a    higher    body    temperature    than    the surrounding   water.   It   can   be   solitary   or   gregarious   and   has   been known    to    perform    seemingly    playful    behaviour    with    flotsam    and jetsam as well as floats used by anglers to present bait. This   is   an   opportunistic   hunter   that   preys   mainly   on   bony   fishes   and cephalopods   throughout   the   water   column,   including   the   bottom.   Most commonly   found   over   food-rich   banks   on   the   outer   continental   shelf,   it makes   occasional   forays   both   close   to   shore   and   into   the   open   ocean to   a   depth   of   over   1000   m.   It   also   conducts   long-distance   seasonal migrations, generally shifting between shallower and deeper water. The   Porbeagle   Shark   is   often   confused   with   the   Mako   Shark   which   has been   known   to   attack   humans   in   tropical   seas,   but   this   variety   is   fuller bodied   and   slower   by   comparison   and   never   recorded   as   attacking humans. Ovoviviparous   litters   of   1   to   5   pups   (or   runts)   every   year   have   been reported.    The    late    embryos    frequently    have    massive    yolk    filled stomachs   owing   to   their   having   consumed   unfertilised   eggs   within   the oviduct. The young are born at a length of 50 to cm. As   the   flesh   is   a   popular   food   in   Europe   it   was   extensively   fished   for with floating long lines west of Ireland. Fossilized    Porbeagle    remains    have    been    found    to    date    1.6    to    5.3 million years ago. The   meat   and   fins   are   highly   valued,   which   has   led   to   a   long   history   of intense   human   exploitation.   However,   this   species   could   not   sustain heavy fishing pressure due to its low reproductive capacity. The   meat   was   sold   fresh,   frozen,   or   dried   and   salted,   and   ranks   among the   most   valuable   of   any   shark.   Most   of   the   demand   came   from Europe,    though    the    United    States    and    Japan    also    imported    this species.   The   fins   were   shipped   to   East   Asia   for   use   in   shark   fin   soup and   the   remainder   utilized   for   production   of   leather,   liver   oil,   and fishmeal. Direct   commercial   fishing,   principally   by   Norwegian   longliners,   led   to stock   collapses   in   the   eastern   North   Atlantic   in   the   1950s,   and   the western   North   Atlantic   in   the   1960s.   Unfortunately,   they   continue   to   be caught   throughout   its   range,   both   intentionally   and   as   a   by-catch.   The International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   (IUCN)   has   added   this species   to   their   ‘RedList’   as   ‘Critically   Endangered’,   extremely   high   risk of   becoming   extinct   in   the   wild,   which   results   in   a   total   ban   on   fishing for this species.

SEASONS

The    Porbeagle    is    a    strong    swimmer    living    in    the    surface layers    of    cold    water    seas,    feeding    on    Mackerel    and    Squid which they follow inshore. They   are   common   around   Europe   except   in   the   North   and   the North   Sea,   remaining   for   long   periods   in   certain   areas   such as deepwater reefs and headlands.
Attains   a   maximum   length   of   3   m   and   c.225   kg   in   weight.   The average   length   in   British   waters   appears   to   be   around   1.8   - 2.4 m.

Remembering    that    this    is    now    a    protected    species.    To    catch sharks,   and   then   release   them   unharmed,   the   general   principals are   the   same   for   all   species,   employing   a   combination   of   drifting or   trolling   with   rubby   dubby   ladled   or   hung   in   a   mesh   net   on   the surface. Offer    more    than    one    bait    on    needle-sharp    hooks    at    different depths   -   with   all   crimps   soldered   for   maximum   strength   -   using   a balloon   as   a   float.   When   the   shark   takes   the   bait   strike   hard   on the   second   run   while   quickly   reeling   in   all   other   baits   to   avoid tangles. Play   the   fish   to   exhaustion   using   a   butt   pad   or   shoulder   harness and   if   necessary   use   a   wide   gaped   gaff   to   pass   a   loop   of   rope   over the   tail   to   boat   the   fish   undamaged   to   disgorge,   photograph   and then return it to the sea unharmed. In   times   gone   by   to   dispatch   sharks   the   method   used   to   be   to   hit the   snout   just   in   front   of   the   eyes   with   a   heavy   priest   then   sever the   vertebrae   behind   the   head   with   a   sharp   knife   or   tie   to   the   boat by its tail so that it cannot swim and it will then drown.
The   Porbeagle   is   well   regarded   as   a   game   fish   by   recreational anglers but there are regulations regarding it’s capture. The   etymology   of   the   word   "porbeagle"   is   obscure.   A   common suggestion    is    that    it    combines    "porpoise"    and    "beagle", referencing   this   shark's   shape   and   tenacious   hunting   habits. Another   is   that   it   is   derived   from   the   Cornish   porth,   meaning "harbor", and bugel, meaning "shepherd". It    can    be    solitary    or    gregarious    and    has    been    known    to perform   seemingly   playful   behaviour   with   flotsam   and   jetsam as well as floats used by anglers to present bait. The   streamlined   shape   and   long   gill   slits   of   the   porbeagle   are adaptations   for   a   fast,   active   lifestyle   and   can   be   found   singly or in groups It   has   physiological   adaptations   that   enable   it   to   maintain   a higher body temperature than the surrounding water. The   Porbeagle   is   an   opportunistic   hunter   that   preys   mainly   on bony   fishes   and   cephalopods   throughout   the   water   column, including   the   bottom.   Most   commonly   found   over   food-rich banks    on    the    outer    continental    shelf,    it    makes    occasional forays   both   close   to   shore   and   into   the   open   ocean   to   a   depth of    over    1000    m.    It    also    conducts    long-distance    seasonal migrations,   generally   shifting   between   shallower   and   deeper water. Only   a   few   shark   attacks   of   uncertain   provenance   have   been attributed    to    the    porbeagle.    It    is    often    confused    with    the Mako   which   has   been   known   to   attack   humans   in   tropical seas,    but    the    Porbeagle    is    fuller    bodied    and    slower    by comparison and never recorded as attacking humans. Ovoviviparous   litters   of   1   to   5   pups   (or   runts)   every   year have    been    reported.    The    late    embryos    frequently    have massive   yolk   filled   stomachs   owing   to   their   having   consumed unfertilised   eggs   within   the   oviduct.   The   young   are   born   at   a length of 50 to cm. As   the   flesh   is   a   popular   food   in   Europe   it   was   extensively fished for with floating long lines west of Ireland. Fossilized   Porbeagle   remains   have   been   found   to   date   1.6   to 5.3 million years ago. The   meat   and   fins   are   highly   valued,   which   has   led   to   a   long history   of   intense   human   exploitation.   However,   this   species could    not    sustain    heavy    fishing    pressure    due    to    its    low reproductive capacity. The   meat   was   sold   fresh,   frozen,   or   dried   and   salted,   and ranks   among   the   most   valuable   of   any   shark.   Most   of   the demand   came   from   Europe,   though   the   United   States   and Japan   also   imported   this   species.   The   fins   were   shipped   to East   Asia   for   use   in   shark   fin   soup   and   the   remainder   utilized for production of leather, liver oil, and fishmeal. Direct      commercial      fishing,      principally      by      Norwegian longliners,   led   to   stock   collapses   in   the   eastern   North   Atlantic in   the   1950s,   and   the   western   North   Atlantic   in   the   1960s. Unfortunately,    they    continue    to    be    caught    throughout    its range,   both   intentionally   and   as   a   by-catch.   The   International Union    for    Conservation    of    Nature    (IUCN)    has    added    this species   to   their   ‘RedList’   as   ‘Critically   Endangered’,   extremely high   risk   of   becoming   extinct   in   the   wild,   which   results   in   a total ban on fishing for this species.

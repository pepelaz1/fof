The Thornback Ray like other rays is a relative of the Shark. Females,   always   the   largest,   grow   to   one   metre   in   ten   years   whilst males take over 15 years to reach this size. The   breeding   cycle   appears   to   govern   the   migration   timing.   In   the spring   the   mature   females   move   into   inshore   waters,   followed   within   a month or so by the mature males. Males   have   pelvic   claspers   by   which   spermatozoa   are   introduced   into the   genital   opening   of   the   female.   Fertilization   is   thus   internal,   but these    fish    are    oviparous,    and    the    eggs    are    laid    in    dark,    oblong, leathery   cases,   with   a   projection   in   each   corner.   These   capsules   are often   numerous   in   the   jetsam   found   on   beaches   in   late   summer   and autumn. In   the   first   days   of   hatching   the   young   ray   does   not   feed   but   subsists on   the   residual   yolk   from   the   egg.   Its   first   food   consists   of   small crustaceans, mainly amphipods and small bottom living shrimps. Distinguished   by   dense   prickles   over   the   entire   back   and   larger   thorns in   the   mid-line   from   mid-disc   to   the   dorsal   fins.   In   sexual   mature specimens   these   thorns   are   very   large,   with   button-like   bases,   known as   'Bucklers',   present   on   the   back   and   sides   of   the   tail   in   males,   and well developed ventrally also in females. The   Thornback   Ray   is   one   of   the   first   species   to   come   inshore   to depths   of   20   metres   for   summer,   retreating   to   deeper   water   (100 metres-plus) in winter. Although   the   name   'Thornback   Ray'   is   widely   used,   it   is   to   some   extent a   book   name   and   Roker   is   most   commonly   used   among   fishermen   in East Anglia, a word derived from the Danish language. This   fish   is   mainly   nocturnal   in   shallow   water   preferring   to   feed   in   calm conditions   either   side   of   slack   water   -   although   they   like   fast   currents lying in shelter on sandbanks. The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   (IUCN)   has   added this   species   to   their   ‘RedList’   as   ‘Near   Threatened’,   likely   to   become endangered    in    the    near    future.    The    ICFA    have    already    placed restrictions   for   Kent   and   Southern   regions.   Always   check   beforehand as fines for landing are substantial. Rays   lie   on   the   bait   before   taking   it,   so   do   not   strike   too   soon.   The   fish will   usually   surface   well   away   from   the   boat   and   may   make   one   last attempt   to   dive   before   giving   in.   Use   a   gaff   or   large   net   to   land   and always   pick   up   near   the   eyes   where   there   are   fewer   thorns,   which although non-venomous, can cause infection. Cut   the   back   of   the   neck   as   soon   as   possible   to   drain   the   blood   which will   otherwise   contaminate   the   flesh   -   and   don't   dispose   of   the   entrails or   frame,   known   as   'banjo',   overboard   as   this   will   clear   the   area   of other rays. When   storing,   pack   'white   to   white'   to   prevent   marking   and   keep   away from other fish to avoid ammonia tainting.

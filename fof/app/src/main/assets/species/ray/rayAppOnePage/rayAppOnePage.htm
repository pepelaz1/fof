RAY, THORNBACK     (Raja clavata)

aka: Roker, Prickler, Longtail, Fairmaid

Very   variable   dorsally,   medium   brown   to   light   grey   with   light brown    to    yellowish    mottling.    Numerous    dark    spots    and yellowish   patches      sometimes   surrounded   with   dark   spots   to form    a    faint    ocellus.    Ventrally    a    pale    cream    colour    with greyish margins.

SEASONS

Its    natural    habitats    are    open    and    shallow    seas.    Found    in shallow   water   on   muddy,   sandy   or   gravel   bottoms,   rarely   on rough   grounds   in   depths   of   10   to   60   m.   It   is   sometimes   seen trapped in large estuarine pools at low tide.
Grows    to    a    maximum    length    of    85    cm    width    61    cm    and attains   a   weight   of   17.25   kg.   The   largest   specimens   are   the females.

The    members    of    this    family,    Rajidae,    are    all    flattened cartilaginous   fishes   with   broad   pectoral   fins   which   give   the body its diamond shape, and a long tail.    Two   genera   are   represented   in   this   area,   Raja   and   Bathyraja comprising   of   some   20   species   which   are   known   in   the   waters of the north-east Atlantic. Their   identification   frequently   presents   many   problems,   which are   compounded   by   differences   in   body   shape   or   development of skin spines within the same species.

FACTS

The Thornback Ray like other rays is a relative of the Shark. Females,   always   the   largest,   grow   to   one   metre   in   ten   years whilst males take over 15 years to reach this size. The   breeding   cycle   appears   to   govern   the   migration   timing. In   the   spring   the   mature   females   move   into   inshore   waters, followed within a month or so by the mature males. Males     have     pelvic     claspers     by     which     spermatozoa     are introduced     into     the     genital     opening     of     the     female. Fertilization   is   thus   internal,   but   these   fish   are   oviparous,   and the    eggs    are    laid    in    dark,    oblong,    leathery    cases,    with    a projection   in   each   corner.   These   capsules   are   often   numerous in the jetsam found on beaches in late summer and autumn. In   the   first   days   of   hatching   the   young   ray   does   not   feed   but subsists    on    the    residual    yolk    from    the    egg.    Its    first    food consists   of   small   crustaceans,   mainly   amphipods   and   small bottom living shrimps. Distinguished    by    dense    prickles    over    the    entire    back    and larger   thorns   in   the   mid-line   from   mid-disc   to   the   dorsal   fins. In   sexual   mature   specimens   these   thorns   are   very   large,   with button-like   bases,   known   as   'Bucklers',   present   on   the   back and   sides   of   the   tail   in   males,   and   well   developed   ventrally also in females. The   Thornback   Ray   is   one   of   the   first   species   to   come   inshore to    depths    of    20    metres    for    summer,    retreating    to    deeper water (100 metres-plus) in winter. Although   the   name   'Thornback   Ray'   is   widely   used,   it   is   to some   extent   a   book   name   and   Roker   is   most   commonly   used among   fishermen   in   East   Anglia,   a   word   derived   from   the Danish language. This   fish   is   mainly   nocturnal   in   shallow   water   preferring   to feed   in   calm   conditions   either   side   of   slack   water   -   although they like fast currents lying in shelter on sandbanks. The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   (IUCN) has   added   this   species   to   their   ‘RedList’   as   ‘Near   Threatened’, likely   to   become   endangered   in   the   near   future.   The   ICFA have    already    placed    restrictions    for    Kent    and    Southern regions.   Always   check   beforehand   as   fines   for   landing   are substantial.

This   fish   is   mainly   nocturnal   in   shallow   water   preferring   to   feed   in calm   conditions   either   side   of   slack   water   -   although   they   like   fast currents lying in shelter on sandbanks. Rays   lie   on   the   bait   before   taking   it,   so   do   not   strike   too   soon.   The fish   will   usually   surface   well   away   from   the   boat   and   may   make one last attempt to dive before giving in. Use   a   gaff   or   large   net   to   land   and   always   pick   up   near   the   eyes where   there   are   fewer   thorns,   which   although   non-venomous,   can cause infection. Cut   the   back   of   the   neck   as   soon   as   possible   to   drain   the   blood which   will   otherwise   contaminate   the   flesh   -   and   don't   dispose   of the   entrails   or   frame,   known   as   'banjo',   overboard   as   this   will   clear the area of other rays. When   storing,   pack   'white   to   white'   to   prevent   marking   and   keep away from other fish to avoid ammonia tainting.

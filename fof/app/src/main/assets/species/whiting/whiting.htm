The   Whiting,   a   member   of   the   Cod   family,   can   be   found   close   inshore from    September    to    January    when    seas    are    reasonably    calm.    The schools   retreat   to   deeper   water   when   the   seas   are   rough   and   up   to 200 metres in summer. This   fish   is   a   voracious   predator,   hunting   small   fish,   crustaceans   and large   quantities   of   its   own   fry   so   catching   adults   actually   helps   to preserve the species! Usual   lifespan   is   five   to   seven   years,   maturing   after   three   or   four   but   it can   live   up   to   13.   Soon   after   hatching,   the   young   fish   (around   3cm.) can be found sheltering among the tentacles of jellyfish. Whiting   form   an   important   part   of   the   diet   of   other,   larger   fish   and   also sea    birds.    Until    the    late    20th    century,    Whiting    was    a    cheap    fish, regarded as food for the poor or for pets. Whiting   was   also   allegedly   used   as   a   filler   in   flour,   beit   wheat,   barley   or rye.    In    times    of    shortages,    particularly    in    the    eighteenth    century, millers   and   bakers   were   commonly   accused   of   using   ground   whiting   to cut   their   flour   as   the   costs   of   the   fish   was   lower   than   that   of   the   actual grain.   The   general   decline   in   fish   stocks   means   it   is   now   more   highly valued. Whiting    and    related    cod    species    are    plagued    by    parasites.    These include   the   cod   worm,   Lernaeocera   branchialis,   a   copepod   crustacean that   clings   to   the   gills   then   entering   the   main   blood   vessel   where   it metamorphoses   into   a   plump,   sinusoidal,   wormlike   body,   with   a   coiled mass of egg strings at the rear. This   fish   feeds   at   dawn   and   dusk   as   well   as   two   hours   either   side   of slack   water.   It   is   most   abundant   at   a   depth   of   30   metres   and   is generally   to   be   found   at   certain   marks   each   season   when   schools   will attack baits enthusiastically if well presented. Remember to watch your rod always not to miss strikes.
WHITING (merlangius merlangus) aka: Channel whiting / Pin whiting (Juveniles) / Merling

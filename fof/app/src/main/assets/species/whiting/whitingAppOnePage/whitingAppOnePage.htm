SEASONS

The   Whiting   is   a   very   common   fish   in   shallow   inshore   waters. It   is   most   abundant   between   30   and   100   m,   exceptionally down to 200m. It   lives   both   in   mid-water   in   these   depths   and   inhabits   sandy as well as muddy bottoms. Most    are    found    in    these    cool    temperate    waters    of    the northern   hemisphere   on   or   close   to   the   continental   shelf.   Few are found in the deep sea and then only near the surface.
Usually   around   30   -   40   cm   but   occasionally   attained   a   length of 70 cm and attains a weight of 3 kg.

The    members    of    the    cod    family    (Gadidae)    are,    with    one exception, all marine fishes. Their   fins   lack   spines,   and   they   usually   have   2   or   3   dorsal and   1   or   2   anal   fins.   A   barbel   on   the   chin   is   normal,   and   they all have small to minute scales. Approximately    100    species    are    known    world-wide,    but    no more than30 live in European waters one of which is Whiting.

FACTS

The   Whiting,   a   member   of   the   Cod   family,   can   be   found   close inshore   from   September   to   January   when   seas   are   reasonably calm.   The   schools   retreat   to   deeper   water   when   the   seas   are rough and up to 200 metres in summer. This     fish     is     a     voracious     predator,     hunting     small     fish, crustaceans   and   large   quantities   of   its   own   fry   so   catching adults actually helps to preserve the species! Usual   lifespan   is   five   to   seven   years,   maturing   after   three   or four   but   it   can   live   up   to   13.   Soon   after   hatching,   the   young fish    (around    3cm.)    can    be    found    sheltering    among    the tentacles of jellyfish. Whiting   form   an   important   part   of   the   diet   of   other,   larger fish   and   also   sea   birds.   Until   the   late   20th   century,   Whiting was a cheap fish, regarded as food for the poor or for pets. Whiting    was    also    allegedly    used    as    a    filler    in    flour,    beit wheat,   barley   or   rye.   In   times   of   shortages,   particularly   in the   eighteenth   century,   millers   and   bakers   were   commonly accused   of   using   ground   whiting   to   cut   their   flour   as   the   costs of    the    fish    was    lower    than    that    of    the    actual    grain.    The general   decline   in   fish   stocks   means   it   is   now   more   highly valued. Whiting   and   related   cod   species   are   plagued   by   parasites. These    include    the    cod    worm,    Lernaeocera    branchialis,    a copepod   crustacean   that   clings   to   the   gills   then   entering   the main    blood    vessel    where    it    metamorphoses    into    a    plump, sinusoidal,   wormlike   body,   with   a   coiled   mass   of   egg   strings at   the   rear.   Although   not   very   nice   they   do   not   cause   any harm to humans.

The   Haddock   is   the   tastiest   of   the   Cod   family,   preferring   seas   that   are relatively   cold.   It   is   to   be   found   throughout   the   North   Sea   and   Atlantic towards   Ireland   but   is   now   becoming   rare   at   the   eastern   end   of   the English Channel and in the south west. Haddock   live   near   the   sea   bed   over   soft   bottoms   and   in   large   schools around    mid-water.    In    higher    latitudes    they    come    inshore    during summer,    retreating    to    deeper    water    in    winter,    though    vice-versa further south. The   most   important   spawning   grounds   are   in   the   waters   off   middle Norway,   near   south-west   Iceland,   and   Georges   Bank.   An   average-sized female   produces   approximately   850,000   eggs,   and   larger   females   can produce   up   to   3   million   eggs   each   year   at   a   depth   of   60-200   metres from   late   February   to   May   -   after   an   intricate   courtship   display   which involves both grunting and sight signals. The   young   fish   can   be   found   sheltering   under   jellyfish   such   as   lion's mane.   This   species   lives   an   average   of   six   to   eight   years   -   though   they can live as long as 13 years. The   haddock   is   easily   recognized   by   a   black   lateral   line   running   along its   white   side   (not   to   be   confused   with   Pollock   which   has   the   reverse, i.e.,   white   line   on   black   side)   and   a   distinctive   dark   blotch   above   the pectoral   fin,   often   described   as   a   "thumbprint"   or   even   the   "Devil's thumbprint"   or   "St.   Peter's   mark".   A   similar   story   to   the   Dory   tells   how the   Haddock   obtained   its   'thumbprint'   markings   -   though   this   species does not inhabit the Mediterranean. In    2010,    Greenpeace    International    has    added    the    Haddock    to    its seafood red list. Haddock and related species are plagued by parasites. Haddock   is   a   very   popular   food   fish,   sold   fresh,   smoked,   frozen,   dried, or to a small extent canned. Fresh   haddock   has   clean,   white   flesh   and   can   be   cooked   in   the   same ways   as   cod.   Freshness   of   a   haddock   fillet   can   be   determined   by   how well   it   holds   together,   as   a   fresh   one   will   be   firm;   also,   fillets   should   be translucent, while older fillets turn chalky (nearly opaque). Unlike    the    related    Cod,    Haddock    does    not    salt    well    and    is    often preserved   by   drying   and   smoking.   Smoked   haddock   naturally   has   an off-white   colour;   it   is   very   often   dyed   yellow,   as   are   other   smoked   fish. Smoked   Haddock   is   the   essential   ingredient   in   the   Anglo-Indian   dish kedgeree as well as other dishes.

HADDOCK (

Melanogrammus aegleanus)

The   haddock   is   a   demersal   species   which   occurs   at   depths from   10   metres   (33   ft)   to   450   metres   (1,480   ft),   although   it is    most    frequently    recorded    at    80    metres    (260ft)    to    200 metres   (660ft).   It   is   found   over   substrates   made   up   of   rock, sand, gravel or shells.
Additional    help    to    identify    this    species    can    be    found    in Google images using the following link;   www.google.com
Dark   greeny-brown   on   the   back,   greyish   silver   on   the   sides, white   ventrally.   A   conspicuous   rounded   black   blotch   between the pectoral base and the black lateral line.
Fishing   for   Haddock   is   best   on   the   drift   using   French   boom   as low   as   possible,   keeping   the   bait   right   down   to   the   sea   bed using    rotten    bottom    to    attach    weight.    Alternatively    use    a Flying    collar    rig    sent    to    the    bottom    and    slowly    retrieved. Wait   for   the   rod   to   bend   before   striking   and   keep   the   main line   tight   whilst   bringing   up   your   catch   and   use   the   net   to land the fish in case it is only lip hooked.
Attains   a   maximum   length   of   76   cm   and   a   maximum   weight of   4.3   kg   although   it's   commercial   length   is   between   38   -   63 cm.   The   largest   recorded   was   caught   off   Iceland,   it   was   112 cm and weighed nearly 17 kg.
Gadiformes   are   an   order   of   ray-finned   fish,   also   called   the Anacanthini,   that   includes   the   cod   and   its   allies.   Many   major food   fish   are   in   this   order.   They   are   found   in   marine   waters throughout   the   world   and   the   vast   majority   of   the   species   are found in temperate or colder regions. They   are   generally   medium-sized   fish,   and   are   distinguished by   the   presence   of   three   dorsal   fins   on   the   back   and   two   on the    underside.    Most    species    have    barbels    on    their    chins, which they use while browsing on the sea floor.
The   Haddock   is   the   tastiest   of   the   Cod   family,   preferring   seas that   are   relatively   cold.   It   is   to   be   found   throughout   the   North Sea   and   Atlantic   towards   Ireland   but   is   now   becoming   rare   at the eastern end of the English Channel and in the south west. The    members    of    the    cod    family    (Gadidae)    are,    with    one exception,    all    marine    fishes.    Most    are    found    in    the    cool temperate   waters   of   the   northern   hemisphere   generally   on   or close to the continental shelf. Few   are   found   in   the   deep   sea   and   then   only   near   the   surface or   in   the   southern   hemisphere.   Their   fins   lack   spines,   and they   usually   have   2   or   3   dorsal   and   1   or   2   anal   fins.   A   barbel on   the   chin   is   normal,   and   they   all   have   small   to   minute scales. Approximately    100    species    are    known    world-wide,    but    no more    than30    live    in    European    waters    one    of    which    is    the Haddock. Haddock   live   near   the   sea   bed   over   soft   bottoms   and   in   large schools    around    mid-water.    In    higher    latitudes    they    come inshore   during   summer,   retreating   to   deeper   water   in   winter, though vice-versa further south. The   most   important   spawning   grounds   are   in   the   waters   off middle   Norway,   near   southwest   Iceland,   and   Georges   Bank. An    average-sized    female    produces    approximately    850,000 eggs,   and   larger   females   can   produce   up   to   3   million   eggs each   year   at   a   depth   of   60-200   metres   from   late   February   to May   after   an   intricate   courtship   display   which   involves   both grunting and sight signals. The   young   fish   can   be   found   sheltering   under   jellyfish   such   as lion's    mane.    This    species    lives    an    average    of    six    to    eight years - though they can live as long as 13 years. The    haddock    is    easily    recognized    by    a    black    lateral    line running   along   its   white   side   (not   to   be   confused   with   Pollock which   has   the   reverse,   i.e.,   white   line   on   black   side)   and   a distinctive   dark   blotch   above   the   pectoral   fin,   often   described as   a   "thumbprint"   or   even   the   "Devil's   thumbprint"   or   "St. Peter's    mark".    A    similar    story    to    the    Dory    tells    how    the Haddock    obtained    its    'thumbprint'    markings    though    this species does not inhabit the Mediterranean. In   2010,   Greenpeace   International   added   the   Haddock   to   its seafood   red   list   but   IUCN   class   it   as   ‘Least   Concern’   meaning it is abundant and widespread! Haddock    and    other    related    cod    species    are    plagued    by parasites. Fresh   haddock   has   clean,   white   flesh   and   can   be   cooked   in the   same   ways   as   cod.   Freshness   of   a   haddock   fillet   can   be determined   by   how   well   it   holds   together,   as   a   fresh   one   will be   firm;   also,   fillets   should   be   translucent,   while   older   fillets turn chalky (nearly opaque). Unlike   the   related   Cod,   Haddock   does   not   salt   well   and   is often    preserved    by    drying    and    smoking.    Smoked    haddock naturally   has   an   off-white   colour;   it   is   very   often   dyed   yellow, as   are   other   smoked   fish.   Smoked   Haddock   is   the   essential ingredient   in   the   Anglo-Indian   dish   kedgeree   as   well   as   other dishes.

Eels   are   distinctive   fishes   by   reason   of   their   elongate   body   form,   and long   dorsal   and   anal   fins,   which   usually   merge   with   the   tail   fin.   None has   pelvic   fins,   and   members   of   several   families   have   no   pectoral   fins. Some   eels   have   scaly   bodies,   but   the   scales   are   small   and   embedded in the skin. The   Conger   feeds   mainly   at   night   over   rocky   sea   beds   during   slack water,   but   it   can   also   be   found   searching   for   food   by   day   in   deep   water over clean sea beds. The   females   are   always   the   biggest   and   studies   show   that   they   can grow up to 41 kilos in under six years. When    conger    eels    are    between    5    and    15    years    old,    their    bodies undergo   a   transformation,   with   the   reproductive   organs   of   both   males and   females   increasing   in   size   and   the   skeleton   reducing   in   mass   and the    teeth    falling    out.    Conger    eels    then    stop    feeding    and    leave European   waters   and   make   the   long   migration   to   the   subtropical   areas of the Atlantic, such as the Sargasso Sea. The   female   Conger   spawns   just   once   in   its   lifetime,   deep   in   the   Atlantic Ocean   between   Gibraltar   and   The   Azores   or   in   the   Mediterranean   at depths   of   2-4,000   metres,   when   it   sheds   3-6   million   eggs.   The   larva   is transparent,   flattened   and   lives   at   the   sea's   surface.   Once   hatched   the larval     drifting     inshore     for     1     or     2     years     before     undergoing metamorphosis   to   a   small   eel   and   begin   to   swim   back   to   European waters,   where   they   live   until   they   reach   maturity   and   then   begin   to migrate to repeat the cycle. Severe    winters,    when    the    sea    temperature    drops    dramatically    are known to kill Conger wholesale. Congers   are   predators   and   can   attack   humans.   In   2013,   a   diver   was attacked   off   Killary   Harbour,Ireland,   at   a   depth   of   25   metres   when   -the eel bit a large chunk from his face. One   species   of   the   conger   eel,   Conger   Myriaster,   is   an   important   food fish in East Asia. It is often served as Japanese Sushi. Fishing    for    congers    is    recorded    in    the    12th-century,    the    Norman taxation   Pipe   Roll   recorded   two   éperquerie   (designated   places   where conger was dried) on Guernsey and one on Sark. Rubby   dubby   on   the   sea   bed   will   attract   Congers   to   the   bait   but   best   to wait   until   second   bite   before   striking   then   wind   up   quickly   to   get   the fish   off   the   bottom.   They   tend   to   spin   whilst   being   brought   to   the surface so a swivel hook can be an advantage. If   returning   them   to   the   sea,   use   bronze   coated   hooks   as   these   will eventually   rust   away   if   they   cannot   be   removed.   A   trace   line   with   a snap swivel is safer and saves time as disgorging can be difficult. To   kill   the   fish,   hit   the   lymph   heart   (situated   behind   the   vent)   with   a heavy   priest   then   sever   the   spinal   chord   just   behind   the   head.   Transfer straight to sack or bin as the skin is very slimy. To   quieten   the   fish   without   killing,   place   your   fingers   in   its   gills   and press   both   thumbs   hard   on   the   back   of   the   neck.   Conger   can   live   for many   hours   out   of   water   so   be   sure   it   is   dead   before   attempting   to remove the hook and try to tire the fish before bringing to the boat.

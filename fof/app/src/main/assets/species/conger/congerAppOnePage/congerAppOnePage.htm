Several   hundred   species   of   eels   are   recognised   world   wide   in about   25   families.   The   majority   are   marine   fishes   distributed in   all   seas,   except   for   the   polar   regions.   Only   4   species,   each belonging     to     separate     family     Anguillidae,     Muraenidae, Congridae     and     Nemichthyidae     are     common     in     northern European   waters.   Several   other   species,   mostly   small,   live   in deep water.

The   conger   is   a   common   fish   on   rocky   shores   and   offshore. Many   young,   small   fish   can   be   caught   in   deep   shore   pools, particularly   those   with   dense   algal   cover   low   down   the   shore. RED LIST

Eels   are   distinctive   fishes   by   reason   of   their   elongate   body form,   and   long   dorsal   and   anal   fins,   which   usually   merge   with the   tail   fin.   None   has   pelvic   fins,   and   members   of   several families   have   no   pectoral   fins.   Some   eels   have   scaly   bodies, but the scales are small and embedded in the skin. The   Conger   feeds   mainly   at   night   over   rocky   sea   beds   during slack   water,   but   it   can   also   be   found   searching   for   food   by day in deep water over clean sea beds. The   females   are   always   the   biggest   and   studies   show   that they can grow up to 41 kilos in under six years. When    conger    eels    are    between    5    and    15    years    old,    their bodies    undergo    a    transformation,    with    the    reproductive organs   of   both   males   and   females   increasing   in   size   and   the skeleton   reducing   in   mass   and   the   teeth   falling   out.   Conger eels   then   stop   feeding   and   leave   European   waters   and   make the   long   migration   to   the   subtropical   areas   of   the   Atlantic, such as the Sargasso Sea. The   female   Conger   spawns   just   once   in   its   lifetime,   deep   in the   Atlantic   Ocean   between   Gibraltar   and   The   Azores   or   in   the Mediterranean   at   depths   of   2-4,000   metres,   when   it   sheds   3- 6   million   eggs.   The   larva   is   transparent,   flattened   and   lives   at the   sea's   surface.   Once   hatched   the   larval   drifting   inshore   for 1   or   2   years   before   undergoing   metamorphosis   to   a   small   eel and   begin   to   swim   back   to   European   waters,   where   they   live until   they   reach   maturity   and   then   begin   to   migrate   to   repeat the cycle. Severe   winters,   when   the   sea   temperature   drops   dramatically are known to kill Conger wholesale. Congers   are   predators   and   can   attack   humans.   In   2013,   a diver   was   attacked   off   Killary   Harbour,   Ireland,   at   a   depth   of 25 metres when the eel bit a large chunk from his face. One    species    of    the    conger    eel,    Conger    Myriaster,    is    an important    food    fish    in    East    Asia.    It    is    often    served    as Japanese Sushi. Fishing    for    congers    is    recorded    in    the    12th-century,    the Norman      taxation      Pipe      Roll      recorded      two      perquerie (designated   places   where   conger   was   dried)   on   Guernsey   and one on Sark. Although   not   on   the   IUCN   Red   List   there   are   restrictions   in some   areas   set   by   the   regional   IFCA   in   particular   North   West, Southern   and   Cornwall.   The   restrictions   could   range   from   a total   ban   to   limited   day   catch   or   restricted   zones   where   there is   natural   habitat   for   spawning   and   home   to   juveniles.   Fines for landing are heavy and best avoided. Rubby   dubby   on   the   sea   bed   will   attract   Congers   to   the   bait but   best   to   wait   until   second   bite   before   striking   then   wind   up quickly   to   get   the   fish   off   the   bottom.   They   tend   to   spin   whilst being   brought   to   the   surface,   so   a   swivel   hook   can   be   an advantage. If   returning   them   to   the   sea,   use   bronze   coated   hooks   as these   will   eventually   rust   away   if   they   cannot   be   removed.   A trace    line    with    a    snap    swivel    is    safer    and    saves    time    as disgorging can be difficult. To   kill   the   fish,   hit   the   lymph   heart   (situated   behind   the   vent) with   a   heavy   priest   then   sever   the   spinal   chord   just   behind the   head.   Transfer   straight   to   sack   or   bin   as   the   skin   is   very slimy. To   quieten   the   fish   without   killing,   place   your   fingers   in   its gills   and   press   both   thumbs   hard   on   the   back   of   the   neck. Conger   can   live   for   many   hours   out   of   water   so   be   sure   it   is dead   before   attempting   to   remove   the   hook   and   try   to   tire   the fish before bringing to the boat. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_conger

Rubby   dubby   on   the   sea   bed   will   attract   Congers   to   the   bait   but wait   until   second   bite   before   striking   -   then   wind   up   quickly   to   get the   fish   off   the   bottom.   Always   best   to   use   a   rig   with   a   swivel   hook as eels tend to rotate during retrieval. The   Conger   is   not   much   esteemed   as   a   food-fish   so   considerable numbers when caught are returned to the sea. If   returning   them   to   the   sea,   use   bronze   coated   hooks   as   these   will eventually   rust   away   if   they   cannot   be   removed.   A   trace   line   with   a snap swivel is safer and saves time as disgorging can be difficult. To   kill   the   fish,   hit   the   lymph   heart   (situated   behind   the   vent)   with a   heavy   priest   then   sever   the   spinal   chord   just   behind   the   head. Transfer straight to sack or bin as the skin is very slimy. To   quieten   the   fish   without   killing,   place   your   fingers   in   its   gills   and press   both   thumbs   hard   on   the   back   of   the   neck.   Conger   can   live for    many    hours    out    of    water    so    be    sure    it    is    dead    before attempting   to   remove   the   hook   -   and   try   to   tire   the   fish   before bringing to the boat.

Found mostly where there are rocks, deep water near rocks,

harbour walls and around piers.

Essential to use heavy nylon or wire trace.

As for boat fishing wait until second bite before striking - then

wind up quickly to get the fish off the bottom and away from

rocks.

Always best to use a rig with a swivel hook as eels tend to rotate

during retrieval.

Restrictions in ICFA regions NW, ST & C

The   Monkfish   gets   its   name   from   the   hooded   appearance   but   though not resembling any shark, its pedigree lies with that marauder. The   word   'Squatina'   is   the   Monkfish's   name   in   Latin,   derived   from   the word   for   Skate;   it   was   made   the   genus   name   for   all   Angel   sharks   by the French zoologist André Duméri in 1806. Evolving   more   in   the   shape   of   the   ray,   living   and   feeding   on   the   sea bed   the   mouth   is   wide   with   large   spiracles   to   aid   breathing   each covered   with   a   large   flap   having   a   flattened   shape.   The   eyes   are   on   top of its head. The   male   Monkfish   has   claspers   on   its   pelvic   fins   and   mates   in   the same way as sharks and rays. The   young   hatch   inside   the   mother's   uterus   and   are   nourished   by   a yolk   sac   until   birth.   The   reproductive   cycle   has   been   estimated   at   2 years   with   ovulation   taking   place   in   spring,   though   this   periodicity   is ill-defined.   The   litter   size   ranges   from   seven   to   25   and   is   correlated with the size of the mother; the young are gestated for 8–10 months. Monkfish    numbers    have    declined    precipitously    across    most    of    its range;   it   is   now   believed   to   be   extinct   in   the   North   Sea   and   most   of the    northern    Mediterranean    it    has    also    become    extremely    rare elsewhere. Due    to    benthic,    near-shore    habits,    individuals    of    all    ages    are susceptible   to   incidental   capture   by   bottom   trawls,   trammel   nets,   and bottom   longlines;   the   low   reproductive   rate   of   this   shark   limits   its capacity to withstand population depletion. Because   of   these   steep   population   declines   and   the   ongoing   threat from    demersal    fisheries,    the    IUCN    has    assessed    the    Monkfish    as 'Critically   Endangered'.   In   2008,   the   Monkfish   also   received   full   legal protection   from   human   activities   in   the   waters   off   England   and   Wales from the coast to 11 km, under the UK Wildlife and Countryside Act. Remains   of   the   Monkfish's   ancestors   have   been   found   among   fossils from   the   Jurassic   age   and   the   skeleton   is   of   cartilage   like   that   of sharks. Humans   have   used   the   Monkfish   for   thousands   of   years.   Ancient   Greek authors,   such   as   Diphilus   and   Mnesitheus,   described   its   meat   as   "light" and    "easily    digestible",    and    Pliny    the    Elder    noted    in    his    Naturalis Historia   (77–79   AD)   that   its   rough   skin   was   valued   by   craftsmen   for polishing   wood   an   ivory.   Aristotle   recorded   elements   of   its   natural history,   including   that   it   bore   live   young,   and   correctly   recognized   that it was a shark despite its resemblance to rays and skates. The   use   of   this   species   for   food   has   continued   into   modern   times;   it   is sold   fresh   or   dried   and   salted,   often   under   the   name   "Monkfish"   (which also   refers   to   the   Goosefishes   of   the   genus   Lophius).   The   Monkfish may   also   be   a   source   for   shark   liver   oil   and   fishmeal.   As   with   the Angler fish, it takes more luck than judgement to catch the Monk. The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   (IUCN)   has   added this   species   to   their   ‘RedList’   as   ‘Critically   Endangered’,   extremely   high risk of becoming extinct in the wild. The   IFCA   /   AT   advise   that   under   the   UK   Wildlife   and   Countryside   Act   it is   listed   to   be   protected   against   killing,   injuring   or   taking   (section   9(1)) on land and up to 3 nautical miles from English coastal base lines.

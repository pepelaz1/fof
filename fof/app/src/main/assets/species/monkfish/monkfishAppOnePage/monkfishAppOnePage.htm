SEASONS

This   summer   visitor   migrates   from   the   south   to   live   in   shallow waters   around   our   shores   found   on   sand   or   mud   in   depths   of 5 - 90 m. It     lies     in     perfect     concealment     usually     partially     buried, although    it    can    swim    powerfully    and    for    relatively    long distances off the bottom.
Most   are   relatively   small.   Attains   a   length   of   183   cms   and   a weight of 32.6 kg. Females are generally larger than males.

FACTS

The   Monkfish   gets   its   name   from   the   hooded   appearance   but though   not   resembling   any   shark,   its   pedigree   lies   with   that marauder. The   word   'Squatina'   is   the   Monkfish's   name   in   Latin,   derived from   the   word   for   Skate;   it   was   made   the   genus   name   for   all Angel sharks by the French zoologist André Duméri in 1806. Evolving   more   in   the   shape   of   the   ray,   living   and   feeding   on the   sea   bed   the   mouth   is   wide   with   large   spiracles   to   aid breathing   each   covered   with   a   large   flap   having   a   flattened shape. The eyes are on top of its head. The   male   Monkfish   has   claspers   on   its   pelvic   fins   and   mates in the same way as sharks and rays. The     young     hatch     inside     the     mother's     uterus     and     are nourished   by   a   yolk   sac   until   birth.   The   reproductive   cycle has   been   estimated   at   2   years   with   ovulation   taking   place   in spring,   though   this   periodicity   is   ill-defined.   The   litter   size ranges   from   seven   to   25   and   is   correlated   with   the   size   of   the mother; the young are gestated for 8–10 months. Monkfish   numbers   have   declined   precipitously   across   most   of its   range;   it   is   now   believed   to   be   extinct   in   the   North   Sea and   most   of   the   northern   Mediterranean   it   has   also   become extremely rare elsewhere. Due   to   benthic,   near-shore   habits,   individuals   of   all   ages   are susceptible   to   incidental   capture   by   bottom   trawls,   trammel nets,   and   bottom   longlines;   the   low   reproductive   rate   of   this shark limits its capacity to withstand population depletion. Because   of   these   steep   population   declines   and   the   ongoing threat   from   demersal   fisheries,   the   IUCN   has   assessed   the Monkfish    as    'Critically    Endangered'.    In    2008,    the    Monkfish also   received   full   legal   protection   from   human   activities   in   the waters   off   England   and   Wales   from   the   coast   to   11   km,   under the UK Wildlife and Countryside Act. Remains   of   the   Monkfish's   ancestors   have   been   found   among fossils   from   the   Jurassic   age   and   the   skeleton   is   of   cartilage like that of sharks. Humans    have    used    the    Monkfish    for    thousands    of    years. Ancient    Greek    authors,    such    as    Diphilus    and    Mnesitheus, described   its   meat   as   "light"   and   "easily   digestible",   and   Pliny the   Elder   noted   in   his   Naturalis   Historia   (77–79   AD)   that   its rough   skin   was   valued   by   craftsmen   for   polishing   wood   an ivory.    Aristotle    recorded    elements    of    its    natural    history, including   that   it   bore   live   young,   and   correctly   recognized that    it    was    a    shark    despite    its    resemblance    to    rays    and skates. The   use   of   this   species   for   food   has   continued   into   modern times;   it   is   sold   fresh   or   dried   and   salted,   often   under   the name   "Monkfish"   (which   also   refers   to   the   Goosefishes   of   the genus   Lophius).   The   Monkfish   may   also   be   a   source   for   shark liver   oil   and   fishmeal.   As   with   the   Angler   fish,   it   takes   more luck than judgement to catch the Monk.

Outside   the   restricted   zones   as   with   the   Angler   fish,   it   takes   more luck than judgement to catch the Monk. Bait   on   a   long   wire   running   ledger   is   most   likely   to   be   taken:   its rough   skin   and   sharp   teeth   will   soon   part   medium   to   light   mono line. Rubby    dubby    or    baiting    up    the    area    may    help    to    attract    the Monkfish   to   your   hook   but   as   it   has   no   value   apart   from   an   edible tail, it is best returned to the sea.

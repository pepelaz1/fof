COD (gadus morhua) a.k.a. Codling (Juveniles)
Cod    has    been    an    important    economic    commodity    in    international markets    since    the    Viking    period    (around    800    AD).    Norwegians travelled   with   dried   cod   and   soon   a   dried   cod   market   developed   in southern Europe. In   1733,   Britain   tried   to   gain   control   over   trade   between   New   England and   the   British   Caribbean   by   imposing   the   Molasses   Act.      In   the   late 20th   and   early   21st   centuries,   fishing   off   the   European   and   American coasts   severely   depleted   stocks   and   become   a   major   political   issue. Iceland re-emerged as a fishing power and entered the Cod Wars Adult   cod   are   active   hunters   and   feeds   on   almost   anything,   varying   its habitat   in   its   constant   search   for   sustenance   and   using   its   chin   barbel and   pelvic   fins   to   detect   food   on   or   just   beneath   the   sea   bed.   Juvenile small   Cod,   ,   are   called   Codling,   of   up   to   3   kilos   are   caught   all   the   year round,   particularly   in   Scotland   and   the   north   east   coast   during   spring and   summer.   The   heavier   fish   move   inshore   between   October   and April. Maturing   at   three   to   four   years,   the   female   sheds   4-5   million   eggs   in late   spring   -   the   main   spawning   ground   being   the   eastern   end   of   the English   Channel.   Growing   to   63   cm   over   three   years   and   usually   living six to ten years, the Cod gains weight throughout its life. Colouration   varies   considerably,   with   specimens   taken   over   a   sandy bottom   showing   pale   grey,   those   from   among   sea   weed,   brown,   green or   even   reddish.   The   Atlantic   cod   can   change   colour   at   certain   water depths.   It   has   two   distinct   colour   phases:   gray-green   and   reddish brown Whilst   mating,   Cod   make   grunting   noises   by   vibrating   the   muscles   of the swim bladder an also to show aggression. Cod   is   popular   as   a   food   with   a   mild   flavour   and   a   dense,   flaky,   white flesh.   Cod   livers   are   processed   to   make   cod   liver   oil,   an   important source of vitamin A, vitamin D, vitamin E, and Omega-3 fatty acids. Cod and related species are plagued by parasites. Young   Atlantic   cod   or   haddock   prepared   in   strips   for   cooking   is   called 'Scrod'.    In    the    United    Kingdom,    Atlantic    cod    is    one    of    the    most common ingredients in fish and chips, along with haddock and plaice. As the Cod is a bottom feeder, tackle should not be more than one metre above the sea bed. Use pirks with or without feathers over wrecks and rocky pinnacles during slack water or when drifting. Large pieces of bait are best with a hook to match. Use a large landing net or a gaff when using multi-hooked rigs and pirks. Gut and wash thoroughly as soon as possible after landing so that the flesh is not tainted by stomach secretions or blood.

COD

 (Gadus morhua) 

aka: Young fish - Codling

Additional   help   to   identify   this   species   can   be   found   in   Google images using this location;   www.google.com
Background   colouring   variable,   greenish   or   sandy   brown   with darker of lighter mottling on back and sides. White ventrally. The lateral line is conspicuously light. Colouration   varies   considerably,   with   specimens   taken   over   a sandy    bottom    showing    pale    grey,    those    from    among    sea weed, brown, green or even reddish. The   Atlantic   cod   can   change   colour   at   certain   water   depths.   It has two distinct colour phases: gray-green and reddish brown
As   the   Cod   is   a   bottom   feeder,   tackle   should   not   be   more   than   one metre above the sea bed. Use   pirks   /   feathers   over   wrecks   and   rocky   pinnacles,   during   slack water   or,   when   fishing   on   the   drift.   Large   pieces   of   bait   are   best   - with a hook to match. Use   a   large   landing   net   or   a   gaff   when   using   multi-hooked   rigs   and pirks.   Gut   and   wash   thoroughly   as   soon   as   possible   after   landing   so that the flesh is not tainted by stomach secretions or blood.

RIG 1: - Leger - Flowing Trace

RIG 2: - Pirk & Feathers

Favour     steep     shelving     beaches     that     have     gullies     and depressions.   Other   areas   are   where   tidal   currents   or   breaking waves   disturb   the   sea   bed.   Night   fishing   can   improve   results. Using   large   mackerel,   squid   or   large   lug   have   provided   good results.

NATURAL HABITAT

DISTRIBUTION

The   cod   is   widely   distributed   in   a   variety   of   habitats   from   the shoreline to well down the continental shelf, depths of 600 m. To   the   south   of   its   range   it   is   found   in   shallow   water   only during   the   winter,   and   there,   as   elsewhere,   it   is   the   younger smaller fish which live close inshore. It    usually    schools    at    least    30    -    40    m    above    the    bottom, although it forages for food on the sea-bed and in mid water.

GROWTH RATE

Historical   records   of   90kg   fish   exist,   but   the   maximum   likely today   is   around   45kg.   Average   cod   measure   about   120cm   and weigh 11kg.

FACTS

The    members    of    the    cod    family    (Gadidae)    are,    with    one exception,    all    marine    fishes.    Most    are    found    in    the    cool temperate   waters   of   the   northern   hemisphere   generally   on   or close to the continental shelf. Few   are   found   in   the   deep   sea   and   then   only   near   the   surface or   in   the   southern   hemisphere.   Their   fins   lack   spines,   and they   usually   have   2   or   3   dorsal   and   1   or   2   anal   fins.   A   barbel on    the    chin    is    normal,    and    they    all    have    small    to    minute scales. Approximately    100    species    are    known    world-wide,    but    no more than 30 live in European waters one of which is the Cod.
Cod     has     been     an     important     economic     commodity     in international    markets    since    the    Viking    period    (around    800 AD).   Norwegians   traded   dried   cod   and   this   market   developed in southern Europe. In    the    late    20th    and    early    21st    centuries,    fishing    off    the European   and   American   coasts   severely   depleted   stocks   and become a major political issue. Iceland   re-emerged   as   a   fishing   power   and   entered   the   Cod Wars to protect itâs fishing grounds. Adult   cod   are   active   hunters   and   feeds   on   almost   anything, varying   its   habitat   in   its   constant   search   for   sustenance   and using   its   chin   barbel   and   pelvic   fins   to   detect   food   on   or   just beneath the sea bed. Small   Cod,   juveniles,   are   called   Codling,   of   up   to   3   kilos   are caught   all   the   year   round,   particularly   in   Scotland   and   the north   east   coast   during   spring   and   summer.   The   heavier   fish move inshore between October and April. Maturing   at   three   to   four   years,   the   female   sheds   4-5   million eggs   in   late   spring   -   the   main   spawning   ground   being   the eastern   end   of   the   English   Channel.   Growing   to   63   cm   over three   years   and   usually   living   six   to   ten   years,   the   Cod   gains weight throughout its life. Whilst    mating,    Cod    make    grunting    noises    by    vibrating    the muscles of the swim bladder an also to show aggression. Cod   and   related   species   are   plagued   by   parasites   which   are not harmful when the fish is cooked. Cod   is   popular   as   a   food   with   a   mild   flavour   and   a   dense, flaky,   white   flesh.   Cod   livers   are   processed   to   make   cod   liver oil,   an   important   source   of   vitamin   A,   vitamin   D,   vitamin   E, and omega-3 fatty acids.

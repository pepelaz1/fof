The   etymology   of   the   name   ‘Dab’   is   unclear,   but   the   modern   English use   seems   to   originate   from   the   Middle   English   'Dabbe'.   It   is   first recorded   in   the   late   16th   century.   The   common   Dab   was   first   named Pleuronectes   limanda   by   Carl   Linnaeus   in   the   1758   10th   edition   of Systema Naturae. The   Dab   is   a   very   common   inshore   fish,   normally   found   in   closely packed   schools   where   there   is   a   strong   current.   This   species,   which lives   up   to   twelve   years,   is   particularly   abundant   in   the   North   Sea.   It spawns 50-150,000 pelagic eggs between January and June. Until   recently   was   mostly   ignored   as   a   commercial   fish,   with   most   dab only   retained   when   they   were   caught   as   by-catch   of   other   targeted species. The   Dab   starts   life   as   a   round   fish   but   when   settling   on   the   sea   bed,   it lies   on   its   left   side   and   the   left   eye   moves   round   to   the   right   side   of   the body.   Although   similar   to   the   plaice,   the   dab   is   narrower,   with   rough- edged   scales   and   a   yellowish   brown   colour   with   dark   blotches   and small   dark   spots.   The   'blind'   side   has   a   smooth   texture,   while   the upper skin feels rough if stroked from tail to head. It   is   a   moderately   important   flatfish   to   the   fishing   industry   and   popular with anglers as relatively easy to catch. In   the   West   Country   it   was   the   custom   to   lance   Dabs   in   shallow   water using   a   trident   spear   which   required   a   keen   eye   and   skill   -   hence   the expression 'a Dab Hand'.
DAB (limanda limanda)  aka: Sand Dab

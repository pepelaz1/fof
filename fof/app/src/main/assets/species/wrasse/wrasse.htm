The   Ballan   Wrasse   is   the   largest   of   the   Wrasse   family   the   smallest member is the Cuckoo Wrasse which resembles the fresh water Carp. All suffer in severe winters and can die from the cold. The   Wrasse   is   a   slow-growing   taking   ten   years   to   reach   1   and   20   kg   to achieve a weight of 4.5. All   Ballan   wrasses   are   female   for   their   first   4   to   14   years   before   a   few change into males. Large Ballan wrasses are almost certainly male. After    six    years    the    female    matures    and    then    between    seven    and fourteen   it   changes   colour   and   sex,   continuing   to   live   to   over   30   years. She   spawns   in   June/July   after   constructing   a   nest   of   algae   in   rock crevices. Wrasse   have   tough,   rubbery   lips   with   many   grooves   and   folds   designed for   tearing   limpets   and   other   shellfish   from   rock   faces.   It   has   powerful grinding    throat    teeth    and    strong    flattened    front    teeth    for    eating molluscs and crustaceans. This   species   is   popular   as   a   food   fish   in   the   Orkney   Islands   and   in Galway,   although   it   is   not   highly   regarded   as   a   food   fish   in   much   of   the UK and Ireland. In   recent   years,   it   has   become   a   popular   catch-and-release   target   for sport   fishermen   using   light   fishing   tackle,   particularly   those   employing soft plastic lures. It   is   also   caught   commercially   and   kept   live   for   transportation   to   fish farms   to   clean   sea   lice   from   farmed   salmon.   However,   the   ICFA   in various    regions    especially    Devon    &    Severn    have    taken    multiple measures    to    manage    the    ‘Live    Wrasse    (pot)    Fishery’    with    closed seasons    whilst    spawning,    pot    limitations,    minimum    and    maximum sizes and voluntary closed areas. This species can also be found in the aquarium trade. Peak   feeding   is   during   August   in   the   daytime   in   so   quick   striking   is necessary   to   get   the   fish   away   from   its   crevice.   The   swim   bladder   is not   connected   to   the   gullet,   so   the   fish   suffer   if   brought   to   the   surface quickly. Float   fishing   will   allow   you   to   vary   depth   and   let   the   bait   sway   in   the current    to    imitate    the    natural    prey.    Presentation    is    important    as Wrasse have excellent eyesight.

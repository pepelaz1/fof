CLASSIFICATION

These    fishes    are    only    representatives    in    shallow    seas    in northern   Europe   of   the   order   of   Zeiformes.   They   belong   to   2 families    Zeidae    and    Caproidae.    All    have    deep    compressed bodies with a large head and a highly producible mouth.

COLOURATION

Mostly   are   brick   red   in   colour.   Dark   yellowish   brown   on   the back   ,   often   with   lighter   yellow   lines   meandering   along   the sides,    silvery    grey    ventrally.    A    conspicuous    black    blotch surrounded   by   a   yellow   ring   on   the   sides.   Anal   and   pelvic   fin membranes black.

NATURAL HABITAT

Comparatively   rare   even   in   the   North   Sea,   Dory   is   most   often found   off   south   and   south   west   coasts   of   Britain   although   this species does range all round eastern Atlantic coasts. The    Dory    is    an    inshore    fish    living    mainly    in    10    -    50    m although   exceptionally   it   is   reported   close   to   the   surface   and as   deep   as   200   m.   It   usually   lives   a   solitary   life   or   is   in   a small school amongst rocks, seaweed and sandy beds.

SEASONS

The   John   Dory,   St   Pierre   or   Peter's   Fish,   refers   to   fish   of   the genus   Zeus,   especially   Zeus   faber,   of   widespread   distribution. It   is   an   edible   benthic   coastal   marine   fish   with   a   laterally compressed   olive-yellow   body   which   has   a   large   dark   spot and   long   spines   on   the   dorsal   fin.   The   John   Doryâ€™s   eye   spot on   the   side   of   its   body   is   used   to   flash   an   'evil   eye'   if   danger approaches    this    also    confuses    prey,    which    can    then    be scooped up in its big mouth. It   is   not   a   strong   swimmer   and   totally   unsuited   to   chasing prey,   the   Dory   hovers   before   slowly   approaching   its   victim head    on    then    extending    its    jaw    forward    in    a    tube-like structure   to   suck   the   fish   in   with   some   water.   The   water   then flows   out   through   the   gills;   the   pre-maxillary   bone,   the   only tooth-bearing bone in this fish, is used to grind the food. Face-on,   the   Dory's   thin   body   is   almost   invisible   and   when close,    the    fish    accelerates    with    a    jerky    motion,    shooting forward   its   large   mouth   to   form   a   kind   of   suction   tube   to capture    its    prey.    Its    large    eyes    at    the    front    of    the    head provide   it   with   binocular   vision   and   depth   perception,   which are important for predators. Legend    has    it    that    the    dark    spots    on    both    sides    are    the thumb    prints    of    St.    Peter    from    when    he    held    the    fish    to extract    tribute    (a    gold    coin)    from    its    mouth.    However, perhaps   it   may   be   considered   unlikely   as   the   apostle   would not   have   encountered   this   species   in   the   fresh   water   Sea   of Galilee! The   name   "St.   Pierre",   or   "Peter's   Fish",   perhaps   explaining why   dories   were   often   referred   to   as   "Peter   Boats",   Saint Peter   being   the   patron   saint   of   fishermen.   A   related   legend says    that    the    dark    spot    on    the    fish's    flank    is    St.    Peter's thumbprint.

John   Dory,   St   Pierre   or   Peter's   Fish,   refers   to   fish   of   the   genus   Zeus, especially   Zeus   faber,   of   widespread   distribution.   It   is   an   edible   benthic coastal   marine   fish   with   a   laterally   compressed   olive-yellow   body   which has   a   large   dark   spot   and   long   spines   on   the   dorsal   fin.   The   John Doryâ€™s   eye   spot   on   the   side   of   its   body   is   used   to   flash   an   'evil   eye'   if danger   approaches   this   also   confuses   prey,   which   can   then   be   scooped up in its big mouth. It   is   not   a   strong   swimmer   and   totally   unsuited   to   chasing   prey,   the Dory    hovers    before    slowly    approaching    its    victim    head    on    then extending   its   jaw   forward   in   a   tube-like   structure   to   suck   the   fish   in with   some   water.   The   water   then   flows   out   through   the   gills;   the   pre- maxillary   bone,   the   only   tooth-bearing   bone   in   this   fish,   is   used   to grind the food. Face-on,   the   Dory's   thin   body   is   almost   invisible   and   when   close,   the fish   accelerates   with   a   jerky   motion,   shooting   forward   its   large   mouth to   form   a   kind   of   suction   tube   to   capture   its   prey.   Its   large   eyes   at   the front   of   the   head   provide   it   with   binocular   vision   and   depth   perception, which are important for predators. Legend   has   it   that   the   dark   spots   on   both   sides   are   the   thumb   prints   of St.   Peter   from   when   he   held   the   fish   to   extract   tribute   (a   gold   coin) from   its   mouth.   However,   perhaps   it   may   be   considered   unlikely   as   the apostle   would   not   have   encountered   this   species   in   the   fresh   water   Sea of Galilee! The   name   "St.   Pierre",   or   "Peter's   Fish",   perhaps   explaining   why   dories were   often   referred   to   as   "Peter   Boats",   Saint   Peter   being   the   patron saint   of   fishermen.   A   related   legend   says   that   the   dark   spot   on   the fish's flank is St. Peter's thumbprint. Catches   are   rare   and   not   actively   targeted.   More   chance   than   skill   will take   a   Dory.   They   have   been   known   to   be   in   same   areas   as   Bream   and will   take   mackerel   strip   or   even   succumb   to   the   use   of   mackerel feathers.

The   Starry   Smooth-hound,   a   member   of   the   shark   family,   and   often confused   with   small   young   Tope   or   the   Less   Common   Smooth-hound that have the similar habitat and distribution. Its   teeth,   unlike   sharks,   are   short,   blunt   and   flattened:   well   developed for   crushing   the   shells   of   molluscs,   crustaceans,   bivalves   and   sea urchins so is not dangerous to man. It   is   found   in   temperate   and   tropical   seas.   Large   schools   live   off   the south   and   south   west   coasts   in   shallow   waters   but   also   as   deep   as 70m    most    common    on    sand    and    gravel    bottoms.    It    is    rather uncommon   in   northern   European   waters   although   it   is   found   as   far north as Denmark. Ovoviviparous;   the   embryos   develop   within   the   mother   where   there   is no   connection   with   the   maternal   membranes   and   nourished   by   the yolk   of   the   egg.   The   female   bears   from   seven   to   fifteen   live   young   in shallow   water   during   the   early   summer,   each   measuring   up   to   30   cm at birth. The period of gestation is approximately 12 months. The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   (IUCN)   in   its   'Red List   of   Threatened   Species'   lists   the   Starry   Smooth-hound   as   "Near Threatened"   meaning   that   they   are   likely   to   become   endangered   in   the future. The   species   is   mainly   nocturnal   when   most   active   but   feeds   incessantly on   or   near   the   sea   bed.   Once   hooked,   the   fish   takes   off   into   tide   so beware of fouling anchor lines.

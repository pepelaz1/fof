FLOUNDER (platichthys flesus)  aka: Fluke / Butt
A   widespread   fish   it   is   taken   mainly   from   the   shore   and   is   found   well up   rivers   and   lakes   connected   to   the   sea   as   it   is   tolerant   of   low   salinity and,   unlike   other   species   of   Pleuronectidae,   it   often   spends   part   of   its life   cycle   in   freshwater   and   regularly   makes   its   way   into   rivers.   In   the British   Isles,   it   has   been   found   as   far   inland   as   Montgomeryshire   on the   River   Severn,   Dinas   Mawddwy   on   the   River   Dovey   and   Garstang   on the River Wyre. Leaving    freshwater    in    the    autumn    they    can    often    be    caught    in estuaries   when   the   first   frosts   occur.   The   fish   then   move   into   deeper water    for    the    winter.    In    the    spring    it    migrates    to    the    spawning grounds,   travelling   at   three   to   four   miles   (five   to   seven   kilometres)   per day and not eating on route. Closely   related   to   the   Plaice,   this   fish   starts   its   life   as   a   normal   larva before   settling   on   the   sea   bed.   Two   thirds   develop   with   their   eyes   on the right side and the remainder on the left. Spawning   takes   place   at   depths   of   20-40   metres   in   the   southern   North Sea   with   the   female   producing   800,000   -2.75   million   pelagic   eggs which   hatch   in   five   to   seven   days.   The   larvae   and   post-larvae   are surface   living.   The   very   young   live   close   inshore   and,   at   about   the   size of a postage stamp, are very abundant in the tidal reaches of rivers. Occasionally   it   interbreeds   with   the   Plaice;   the   hybrid   is   intermediate in form between the two species. Small   bony   thorns   cause   the   skin   to   be   rough   near   the   head   and   at   the base of the fins. It   is   reported   that   by   accident   it   has   been   introduce   into   the   United States and Canada through transport in ships ballast water. For   angling   although   active   at   night   in   calm   summertime   conditions, the   Flounder   is   caught   in   greater   numbers   in   tidal   waters   from   autumn to mid-winter. A   white   celluloid   spoon   is   the   most   suitable   lure   in   weak   currents   and   a running   leger   at   other   times.   The   best   bait   is   lug   or   rag   worm   for coastal   fishing   and   large   bunches   of   harbour   rag   in   estuaries   and rivers.   Fresh   water   tackle   is   ideal   for   estuaries,   mudflats   and   tidal reaches.

LANDING SIZES (MINIMUM)

FLOUNDER  (Platichthys flesus)

aka: Fluke / Butt

Additional   help   to   identify   this   species   can   be   found   in   Google images at the following location;   http://www.google.co.uk
Dull    brown,    even    greenish-brown    with    indistinct    reddish blotches on the eyed side; blind side opaque and dull white. See Facts for myth on colouring.
A   white   celluloid   spoon   is   the   most   suitable   lure   in   weak   currents and   a   running   ledger   at   other   times.   Though   active   at   night   in   calm summertime   conditions,   the   Flounder   is   caught   in   greater   numbers in tidal waters from autumn to mid-winter. The   best   bait   is   lug   or   rag   worm   for   coastal   fishing   and   large bunches   of   harbour   rag   in   estuaries   and   rivers.   Fresh   water   tackle is ideal for estuaries, mudflats and tidal reaches.

SHORE FISHING

Use   very   light   tackle   loaded   with   local   worm   or   squid   /   mackerel. Whilst   fishing   occasionally   gently   move   bait   around   to   search   for the fish. Using   a   two   or   three   hook   will   increase   your   catch.   Increasing   the trace   line   to   15   lbs   will   stop   the   fish   from   grinding   through   line   on being caught. When   using   small   pieces   of   peeler   crab   increase   the   size   of   hook from 2 to 1/0 Fresh    water    tackle    is    ideal    for    estuaries,    mudflats    and    tidal reaches. .

SEASONS

It   is   an   active   swimming,   schooling   fish   in   inshore   waters, commonly   entering   estuaries   and   penetrating   upstream   into almost   freshwater,   a   habitat   of   the   young   especially.   Although it   may   be   captured   in   offshore   waters,   it   is   usually   found   in close   proximity   to   reefs.   It   is   to   some   extent   a   migratory   fish, the   North   Sea   populations,   in   particular,   being   summertime migrants from the south or south-west.
Attains 51 cm in length and a weight of 3 kg..

A   large   family   (Pleuronectidae)   of   flatfishes   which   all   have both   eyes   on   the   right   side   of   the   body,   the   left   eye   moving over    the    head    during    metamorphosis    from    post    larva    to bottom   living   young.   Reversed   examples,   those   which   have both eyes on the left side are very common in Flounders. Eight   species   are   known   from   northern   European   seas;   Plaice, Dab,    Lemon    sole,    Witch,    Long    Rough    Dab,    Halibut    and Flounder.

FACTS

A   widespread   fish   it   is   taken   mainly   from   the   shore   and   is found   well   up   rivers   and   lakes   connected   to   the   sea   as   it   is tolerant     of     low     salinity     and,     unlike     other     species     of Pleuronectidae,    it    often    spends    part    of    its    life    cycle    in freshwater   and   regularly   makes   its   way   into   rivers.   In   the British     Isles,     it     has     been     found     as     far     inland     as Montgomeryshire   on   the   River   Severn,   Dinas   Mawddwy   on   the River Dovey and Garstang on the River Wyre. Leaving   freshwater   in   the   autumn   they   can   often   be   caught   in estuaries   when   the   first   frosts   occur.   The   fish   then   move   into deeper   water   for   the   winter.   In   the   spring   it   migrates   to   the spawning   grounds,   travelling   at   three   to   four   miles   (five   to seven kilometres) per day and not eating on route. Closely    related    to    the    Plaice,    this    fish    starts    its    life    as    a normal    larva    before    settling    on    the    sea    bed.    Two    thirds develop   with   their   eyes   on   the   right   side   and   the   remainder on the left. Spawning    takes    place    at    depths    of    20-40    metres    in    the southern   North   Sea   with   the   female   producing   800,000   -2.75 million   pelagic   eggs   which   hatch   in   five   to   seven   days.   The larvae   and   post-larvae   are   surface   living.   The   very   young   live close   inshore   and,   at   about   the   size   of   a   postage   stamp,   are very abundant in the tidal reaches of rivers. Occasionally    it    interbreeds    with    the    Plaice;    the    hybrid    is intermediate in form between the two species. The   underside   is   white   –   which,   according   to   Arab   legend, occurred   when   Moses   was   cooking   a   flatfish   and,   with   only one   side   browned,   he   ran   out   of   oil.   After   throwing   it   back into   the   sea   the   fish   came   back   to   life,   thereafter   passing down this colouring to its descendants! It   is   reported   that   by   accident   it   has   been   introduce   into   the United   States   and   Canada   through   transport   in   ships   ballast water.

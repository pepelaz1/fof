SEASONS

Turbot   are   very   common   in   shallow   water   from   just   below   the shoreline   to   about   80   m   on   shell,   gravel   and   sand   bottoms. Young    fish    live    in    shallower    water    than    adults,    which    are particularly   common   on   offshore   banks.   The   young   of   less than   1   year   are   common   in   the   breakers   and   tidal   pools   of sandy shores.
Grows   to   a   maximum   length   of   1   m   and   a   length   of   0.50   - 0.80    m    is    more    usual.    A    weight    of    around    5    -    12    kg    is reached. Females are larger than females.

Turbot    are    part    of    a    small    family,    Scophthalmidae,    of flatfishes   which   have   both   eyes   on   the   left   side   of   the   head, reversed specimens are rare. During   larvae   development   the   right   eye   moves   from   the   right side of the head and comes to rest close to the left side.

FACTS

The   Turbot   is   the   largest   flatfish   after   the   Halibut   reaching   up to   25   kilos   in   weight.   A   larger   relative   of   the   Brill,   which   has   a similar    body    shape    and    smooth    skin,    the    Turbot    has    a covering of bony tubercles. The   word   comes   from   the   Old   French   'Tourbout',   which   in   turn is   thought   to   be   a   derivative   of   the   Latin   'Turbo'   (spinning top)   a   possible   reference   to   its   shape.   Another   possible   origin of   the   Old   French   word   is   from   Old   Swedish'   'Törnbut',   from Törn meaning thorn". The     fish     can     assume     the     colour     and     pattern     of     the surrounding   sea   bed   to   make   it   well   camouflaged   whilst   lying half   buried   on   the   down-tide   brow   of   deep-water   sandbanks. The   tidal   current   sweeps   small   fish   and   sandeel   towards   its waiting jaws. The    female,    spawns    around    nine    million    pelagic    eggs    on gravel   bottoms   in   10-80   metres   of   water   and   the   young   start their life on the sea bed at four to six months. The   prime   area   for   the   mature   fish   is   the   central   North   Sea although   this   is   the   close   to   the   extremity   of   its   range   in northern   European   waters   and   rapidly   becomes   scarce   further to the north. This   is   one   of   the   most   valuable   of   marine   food   fishes   and   is ranked by many as the finest flavoured. Usually    acquired    through    trawling    Turbot    are    farmed    in Bulgaria,    France,    Spain,    Portugal,    Romania,    Turkey,    Chile, Norway, and China. Early   reference   to   the   Turbots   reputation   can   be   found   in   a satirical   poem   (The   Emperor's   Fish)   by   Juvenal,   a   Roman   poet of   the   late   1st   and   early   2nd   centuries   A.D.,   suggesting   this fish was "a delicacy in the Roman empire". In   Turkey,   where   the   fish   is   popular   and   expensive,   it   is   called 'Kalkan'   meaning   shield,   from   the   fish's   resemblance   to   such an item. The   IUCN   has   classified   this   species   as   "Vulnerable"   in   its 'Red   List   of   Threatened   Species’   meaning   that   it   is   at   a   high risk   of   becoming   endangered   however   the   IFCA   and   AT   allow landings as long as it is above the minimum length.

This   species   is   classified   in   the   Red   List   as   ‘Vulnerable’   and   there are   landing   size   restrictions   published   by   the   Anglers   Trust   and   the IFCA. To   attract   Turbot,   trot   bait   down-tide   over   sandbanks,   retrieving and keeping it on the move during slack water. When   hooked   the   turbot   can   place   heavy   strain   on   both   tackle   and angler   so   steady   pumping   is   necessary   to   raise   it   to   the   surface. Use a large landing net or gaff to land. To   keep   the   flesh   white   drain   the   blood   by   making   an   incision   in   its tail to sever the main artery.

The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   publishes a   European   Red   List   providing   a   review   of   the   conservation status   of   European   species   identifying   those   species   that   are threatened    with    extinction    at    the    regional    level,    so    that appropriate   conservation   action   can   be   taken   to   improve   their status. The Dog fish conservation status is  ‘Least Concern’
The   Inshore   Fishery   and   Conservation   Authorities   (IFCA)   have considerable   responsibility   for   managing   fish   stocks   out   to   6 miles   from   the   shore.   While   the   IFCA's   still   have   to   adhere   to the   rules   laid   down   by   the   European   Union   Common   Fisheries Policy   they   are   able,   through   bylaws,   to   set   minimum   landing sizes   that   exceed   the   European   Union's   and   apply   to   all   fish retained including those caught by recreational anglers.

Dogfish, Lesser Spotted

(Scyliorhinus canicula)

aka:   

Doggy,   

Rough   

hound,   

Flake,   

Rock   

Eel,

Morgay, Small spotted catshark

Almost   any   bait   however   presented   will   attract   Dogfish.   The   type of rig is not a factor. When   removing   hook,   hold   the   head   and   tail   together   in   one   hand as   the   fish   has   a   nasty   habit   of   curling   round   the   wrist   causing painful    grazes    -    and    be    prepared    for    it    to    regurgitate.    Also remember   to   keep   this   fish   separate   from   other   fish   in   your   bag   to avoid the risk of tainting them with ammonia.

RIG 1: - Ledger - Running Rotten bottom

RIG 2: - Ledger Running

As   for   boat   fishing   if   this   species   are   around   they   are   likely   to take any bait however presented.

NATURAL HABITAT

DISTRIBUTION

It   inhabits   over   rocks,   wrecks,   gravel,   sandy   bottoms   and even mud. Almost everywhere.

GROWTH RATE

Rarely   grows   to   a   length   of   1   m,   more   usually   60   -70   cm. Weights of 1,7 kg are about maximum.

FACTS

The   dogfishes   belong   to   one   of   the   largest   families   of   sharks, Scyliorhinidae,   which   comprises   about   60   species.   Most   are small,    shallow-water    sharks    with    two    dorsal    fins    placed towards   the   tail.   And   a   long   tail   fin.   their   build   accords   with their   bottom-life   style.   Many   are   boldly   marked   or   coloured including      the      3      northern      European      species      dogfish, Nursehound and Black-mouthed dogfish.
The   Dogfish   is   a   small   spotted   Catshark   that   is   often   difficult to   avoid   catching   when   fishing   along   some   parts   of   the   coast with   small   or   medium   sized   bait   in   waters   of   three   metres   or more. A   common   bottom-living   shark   which   occurs   in   depths   of   3   to 110 m and less commonly down to 400m. The   Dogfish   is   mostly   active   at   night,   roaming   between   the sea   bed   and   mid-water   in   search   of   food.   It   has   even   been seen   'asleep'   during   daytime,   but   due   to   its   numbers,   many are taken in the daylight hours. Reproduction   continuous   throughout   the   year,   but   egg-laying takes   place   in   shallow   water   mainly   between   November   and July.   They   are   oviparous,   the   female   lays   seven   to   eight   eggs in   pairs,   and   each   enclosed   in   a   horny   transparent   yellow- brown   capsule   some   5   cm   long   by   2-3   cm   wide,   often   called   a mermaid's    purse,    which    have    a    springy    filament    on    each corner   that   attaches   to   sea   weed   and   other   objects   on   the sea   bed.   Incubation   lasts   for   5   to   11   months   then   hatch   at   a length   of   about   10   cm   and   sexual   maturity   is   attained   at   a length of about 50 cm. Almost   any   bait   however   presented   will   attract   Dogfish.   The type of rig is not a factor. When   removing   hook,   hold   the   head   and   tail   together   in   one hand   as   the   fish   has   a   nasty   habit   of   curling   round   the   wrist causing   painful   grazes   -   and   be   prepared   for   it   to   regurgitate. Also   remember   to   keep   this   fish   separate   from   other   fish   in your bag to avoid the risk of tainting them with ammonia. The    skin    of    this    species    is    highly    abrasive    and    before sandpaper   came   on   the   scene   had   been   used   as   a   substitute for     pumice,     by     cabinet     makers,     French     polishers     and alabaster sculptors. Although   at   one   time   valueless   it   is   now   marketed   as   Huss, Rock   salmon,   Rock   eel   or   Sweet   William   and   forms   a   small but valuable fishery. In   other   parts   of   its   range   it   is   occasionally   baked   or   used   in fish   soup   but   the   fact   that   these   must   be   skinned   before   they can     be     filleted     discourages     commercial     fishermen     from catching this species.

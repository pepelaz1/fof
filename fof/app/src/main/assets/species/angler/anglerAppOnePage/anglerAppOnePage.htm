CLASSIFICATION

Three   families   are   represented   in   Northern   European   waters: 2   shallow   water   Anglers   (Lophiidae)   and   2   deep-sea   species (Himantolophidae    and    Ceratiidae),    both    of    which    are    very rare.

COLOURATION

Very    variable    on    the    back,    ranging    from    reddish-brown    to greeny   -   grey   with   irregular   markings.   Ventrally,   dead   white with   distinct   black   edges   to   the   pelvic   fins   and   dusky   pectoral and anal edges.

GROWTH RATE

Attains   a   maximum   length   of   2m   and   a   weight   of   c.   41kg. Specimens in excess of 1.20m are uncommon.

NATURAL FOOD & BAIT

A   common   bottom   living   fish   from   2-3m   below   tide   mark although   it   is   most   common   below   18m.   It   lives   on   sandy, shell,    or    gravel    bottoms    and    is    found    less    abundantly    on muddy or rough grounds.

RED LIST

The   Angler   gets   its   name   from   the   fishing   rod   shaped   tapered dorsal   fin   ray   protruding   from   its   snout   which   it   raises   and waves   about.   The   fleshy   lure   at   the   end   gives   the   impression of a small fish in difficulties. Lying   half   submerged   in   sand   or   sea   weed   and   assuming   the colour    of    its    surroundings    the    Angler    entices    its    prey    to within    striking    distance,    whereupon    it    engulfs    the    hapless creature   with   an   enormous   mouth   that   hinges   back   like   a snake’s. Most   common   on   the   south   and   south   west   coasts,   the   Angler is   rarely   found   past   the   eastern   end   of   the   English   Channel. The    species    migrates    inshore    in    the    autumn    returning    to deep   water   in   March   and   June   to   spawn   in   depths   of   around 1,500 metres. The   young   fish   swim   freely,   helped   by   their   long   fin   rays which   reduce   in   size   as   they   grow.   On   reaching   a   length   of 8cm.   the   Angler   goes   down   to   the   sea   bed   -   so   stories   of   it coming    to    the    surface    to    drag    down    seabirds    are    highly improbable! The   Angler   is   sometimes   confused   with   the   Monkfish   which   is roughly   the   same   shape.   Noted   as   the   first   source   of   medical insulin,   the   Angler   has   a   soft,   bony   skeleton   whereas   the Monk is cartilaginous. Because   the   Angler   lives   by   luring   its   prey,   it   is   usually   only caught when bottom fishing for other species. When   handling   be   aware   of   its   sharp   teeth   as   it   can   live   for several hours out of water.

LANDING SIZES (MINIMUM)

The   International   Union   for   Conservation   of   Nature   publishes a   European   Red   List   providing   a   review   of   the   conservation status   of   European   species   identifying   those   species   that   are threatened    with    extinction    at    the    regional    level,    so    that appropriate   conservation   action   can   be   taken   to   improve   their status. The Angler conservation status is  ‘Least Concern’
The   Inshore   Fishery   and   Conservation   Authorities   (IFCA)   have considerable   responsibility   for   managing   fish   stocks   out   to   6 miles   from   the   shore.   While   the   IFCA's   still   have   to   adhere   to the   rules   laid   down   by   the   European   Union   Common   Fisheries Policy   they   are   able,   through   bylaws,   to   set   minimum   landing sizes   that   exceed   the   European   Union's   and   apply   to   all   fish retained including those caught by recreational anglers.

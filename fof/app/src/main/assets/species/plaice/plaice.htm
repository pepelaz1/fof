Plaice   are   one   of   the   most   important   flatfish   in   north   European   fishing industry   and   heavy   fishing   has   resulted   in   large   plaice   very   rare   for   it is   a   long-lived   species   becoming   sexually   mature   at   3   to   7   years (females) and 2 to 6 years (males) and living up to 20 years. Plaice     travel     long     distances     by     leaving     the     sea     bed     and drifting/swimming   down   stream   with   the   current   until   the   next   slack tide. During   day   time   they   remain   stationary   for   long   periods   covering   itself with   bottom   sediment   and   changes   colour   and   markings   to   match   its surroundings, but the orange spots always remain visible. They   can   survive   low   salt   concentrations   and   may   occur   in   some   cases in brackish water or even in fresh water. When   spawning   in   late   winter   between   January   and   March,   the   female lays   up   to   500,000   eggs,   always   in   an   area   where   prevailing   currents will   move   the   larvae   towards   sandy   nursery   beaches.   The   eggs   float   on the    surface    initially,    hatching    in    10    -20    days    depending    on    the temperature.   The   larvae   and   post-larvae   are   also   surface   living   for between 4 to 6 weeks, after which, it becomes bottom living. At   first,   as   with   all   flatfish,   life   for   the   Plaice   begins   as   a   round   fish with   its   eyes   on   each   side   of   the   head.   Gradually   it   develops   to   lie   on one   side   and   flatten   out   with   the   underside   eye   moving   round   to   the top. Plaice   grow   slowly   for   the   first   three   years,   and   then   in   the   fourth, substantially increase their size and weight. Plaice,   along   with   the   other   major   demersal   fish   in   the   North   Sea   such as   Cod,   Monkfish   and   Sole,   is   listed   by   the   ICES   International   Council for    the    Exploration    of    the    Sea    as    "Outside    safe    biological    limits" meaning that they have a high risk of depletion. In   2010,   Greenpeace   International   has   added   the   European   plaice   to its seafood red list. Fishing   for   Plaice   requires   using   some   form   of   attractor   e.g.   spoon   / beads    best    mainly    during    the    day    when    they    feed.    Being    highly inquisitive they will go for attractors just above the bait. This   fish   usually   schools,   so   hooking   one   means   good   chances   of others. Generally, the larger fish live in deeper waters. Drift   bottom   legering   over   clean   sand   is   helpful   to   find   schools.   Do   not strike   too   soon.   Give   Plaice   time   to   take   the   bait   and   always   use   a large landing net as it may only be lip hooked.

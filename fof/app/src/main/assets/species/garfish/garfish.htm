The   Garfish,   shaped   like   a   miniature   swordfish,   is   also   known amongst    other    names    as    Mackerel    Guard;    the    latter    possibly because   it   tends   to   swim   around   the   fringes   of   Mackerel   schools 'on guard'. Garfish   live   up   to   18   years,   usually   in   migratory   schools   with Mackerel.   They   feed   during   the   day   -   and   indeed   are   often   caught by foul hooking when feathering for Mackerel. Good sport on light tackle when float fishing. Spawning   in   coastal   waters   in   May/June,   the   eggs   (from   1-35,000 per   female)   have   long   filaments   by   which   they   attach   to   floating sea weed. Rare   examples   reach   over   1kg   but   all   provide   excellent   sport, often   leaping   out   of   the   water   when   hooked   -   a   ploy   they   use   to escape predators such as Tunney and Dolphins. The   Garfish   is   not   commercially   exploited,   sometimes   caught   as by-catch,   mainly   in   fixed   nets   along   the   coast   in   shallow   waters, although   its   flesh   is   of   good   quality   they   are   eaten   boiled,   fried, baked,   barbecued   or   smoked.   Having   unusual   green   bones   (due   to the   presence   of   biliverdin)   this   discourages   many   people   from eating them, but the green colour is harmless.
GARFISH (belone belone)  aka: Garpike / Long Nose /Snipe eel / Spearfish / Mackerel Guard

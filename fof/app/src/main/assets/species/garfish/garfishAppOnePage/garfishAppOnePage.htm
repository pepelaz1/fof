CLASSIFICATION

Two   similar-looking   fishes   which   belongs   to   separate   families (Belonidae    and    Scomberesocidae).    Both    are    surface    living predatory   fish   with   a   long,   slender   body   and   elongated   jaws. The     Garfish     are     mainly     warm     water     temperate     fishes, although   some   live   in   tropical   fresh   waters.   Numerous   species are    known    worldwide,    many    or        them    closely    alike    in appearance, and most live in shallow inshore waters.

COLOURATION

A    beautiful    clear    green    above,    shading    suddenly    to    bright silver with a yellowish tinge on the lower sides and belly.

NATURAL HABITAT

A    surface    living    mainly    offshore    species    which    comes    into shallow   waters   of   northern   Europe   in   late   spring   and   can   be found   close   inshore   throughout   summer   and   autumn.   North   of the British Isles it is a rare straggler.

SEASONS

The   Garfish,   shaped   like   a   miniature   swordfish,   is   also   known amongst   other   names   as   Mackerel   Guard;   the   latter   possibly because    it    tends    to    swim    around    the    fringes    of    Mackerel schools 'on guard'. A    surface    living    mainly    offshore    species    which    comes    into shallow   waters   of   northern   Europe   in   late   spring   and   can   be found   close   inshore   throughout   summer   and   autumn.   North   of the British Isles it is a rare straggler. Spawning   in   coastal   waters   in   May/June,   the   eggs   (from   1- 35,000   per   female)   have   long   filaments   by   which   they   attach to floating sea weed. Garfish   live   up   to   18   years,   usually   in   migratory   schools   with Mackerel. Rare examples reach over 1kg. They   feed   during   the   day   and   indeed   are   often   caught   by   foul hooking when feathering for Mackerel. It   is   a   fine   sporting   fish   using   a   fresh   water   spinning   rod   and the   lightest   tackle   to   give   the   best   chance   of   catching   and playing   the   fish,   often   leaping   out   of   the   water   when   hooked a    ploy    they    use    to    escape    predators    such    as    Tunney    and Dolphins. Presentation   of   bait   is   important   as   the   Garfish   can   see   it   and rubby   dubby   ladled   onto   the   surface   of   the   water   at   intervals helps to attract schools. The   Garfish   is   not   commercially   exploited,   sometimes   caught as   a   by-catch,   mainly   in   fixed   nets   along   the   coast   in   shallow waters.   Although   its   flesh   is   of   good   quality   they   are   eaten boiled,   fried,   baked,   barbecued   or   smoked.   Having   unusual green     bones     (due     to     the     presence     of     biliverdin)     this discourages   many   people   from   eating   them,   but   the   green colour is harmless.

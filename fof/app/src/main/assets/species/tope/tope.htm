The   Tope   is   a   true   small   Shark,   with   stunning   emerald   green   oval- shaped   eyes   and   black   irises.   It   has   no   swim   bladder,   using   its   fins   to maintain balance. Its   reproduction   is   ovoviviparous.   In   water   depths   of   40   to   100   m deep,   during   the   late   spring   or   early   summer,   the   female   bears   up   to 40   live   young   usually   35cms   in   length.   Large   heavier   females,   still carrying their young, are to be caught in late May or early June. The   Tope   is   a   very   fast   and   powerful   swimmer,   reaching   speeds   of   up to   25   mph.   It   lives   in   small   schools   and   is   a   voracious   nocturnal   feeder, seeking out schools of other fish especially Mackerel. The   school   shark   is   a   migratory   species   and   deep-sea   cables   and   the magnetic    field    caused    by    the    current    flow    may    disrupt    migration routes.   Tope   tagged   in   the   United   Kingdom   have   been   recovered   in   the Azores, the Canary Islands, and Iceland. Small   Tope   can   be   eaten,   and   their   flesh   is   similar   to   the   dogfishes,   but large   Tope   is   rank   smelling   and   tough.   Its   skin   is   very   rough   (it   was used   as   a   fine   abrasive   by   French   polishers   and   cabinet   makers   before sandpaper). Laboratory   tests   on   its   liver   showed   that   it   was   higher   in   Vitamin   A content   than   any   other   fish   tested.   After   this   discovery,   it   became   the subject   of   a   much   larger-scale   fishery   which   developed   as   a   result   of the   high   prices   obtainable   for   the   fish   and   its   liver.   It   became   the   main source   of   supply   for   vitamin   A   in   the   United   States   during   World   War II,   but   was   overexploited,   populations   were   reduced,   and   the   numbers of fish caught dwindled The   IUCN   has   classified   this   species   as   "Vulnerable"   in   its   'Red   List   of Threatened   Species’   meaning   that   it   is   at   a   high   risk   of   becoming endangered.    In    2010;    Greenpeace    International    added    the    school shark to its seafood red list. The   IFCA   and   AT   have   placed   a   national   restriction   on   landing   Tope from   a   boat,   which   includes   kayaks   and   are   legally   required   to   be released   as   soon   as   possible   after   capture.   as   ordained   by   the   Tope (Prohibition of Fishing Order) 2008. The   Tope's   teeth   are   designed   for   cutting   not   crushing   so   if   caught accidentally watch your fingers when disgorging! Hand-haul   on   board   by   holding   the   pectoral   fin   and   tail   or   use   a   large net.   Do   not   gaff   and   remember   that   the   male   has   two   claspers   it   uses in   copulation,   so   care   should   be   taken   to   avoid   grasping   them   as   they can    inflict    a    painful    wound.    You    must    return    them    to    the    sea unharmed.

TOPE  

(Galeorhinus galeus)

Additional    help    to    identify    this    species    can    be    found    in Google images using the following link;   www.google.com
Uniformly   grey,   or   greyish   brown   on   the   sides   and   back   white ventrally.   The   outer   margin   of   the   pectoral   fin   is   light   on   the   dorsal surface.
Rubby   dubby   mix   on   the   sea   bed   helps   to   attract   the   Tope   to bait.   Use   a   strong   nylon   or   wire   trace   as   the   fish   will   easily bite through line. At   first   allow   the   fish   to   run   with   the   bait   then   strike   quickly on   the   second   run   to   ensure   the   hook   is   not   swallowed.   Once hooked,   it   may   make   long   runs,   especially   when   near   the boat so use a reel with plenty of line. The   Tope's   teeth   are   designed   for   cutting   not   crushing   so watch your fingers when disgorging! Hand-haul   on   board   by   holding   the   pectoral   fin   and   tail   or   use a   large   net.   Do   not   gaff   and   remember   that   the   male   has   two claspers   it   uses   in   copulation   so   care   should   be   taken   to   avoid grasping   them   as   they   can   inflict   a   painful   wound.   Return   to the sea unharmed.
Grows   to   a   maximum   length   of   1.67   and   a   length   of   1.2   m   is more usual. A weight of around 33.9 kg is reached.
This   is   the   largest   order,   Carcharinidae,   of   living   sharks   with possibly 80-100 members.   They    are    most    abundant    in    tropical    and    warm    temperate seas,   those   which   occur   in   temperate   seas,   those   which   occur in   temperate   seas,   two   only   in   northern   Europe,   are   often seasonal migrants.
The   Tope   is   a   true   small   Shark,   with   stunning   emerald   green oval-shaped   eyes   and   black   irises.   It   has   no   swim   bladder, using its fins to maintain balance. Its   reproduction   is   ovoviviparous.   In   water   depths   of   40   to 100   m   deep,   during   the   late   spring   or   early   summer,   the female   bears   up   to   40   live   young   usually   35   cms   in   length. Large   heavier   females,   still   carrying   their   young,   are   to   be caught in late May or early June. The    Tope    is    a    very    fast    and    powerful    swimmer,    reaching speeds   of   up   to   25   mph.   It   lives   in   small   schools   and   is   a voracious   nocturnal   feeder,   seeking   out   schools   of   other   fish especially Mackerel. The   school   shark   is   a   migratory   species   and   deep-sea   cables and   the   magnetic   field   caused   by   the   current   flow   may   disrupt migration   routes.   Tope   tagged   in   the   United   Kingdom   have been    recovered    in    the    Azores,    the    Canary    Islands,    and Iceland. Small   Tope   can   be   eaten,   and   their   flesh   is   similar   to   the dogfishes,   but   large   tope   is   rank   smelling   and   tough.   Its   skin is    very    rough    (it    was    used    as    a    fine    abrasive    by    French polishers and cabinet makers before sandpaper). Laboratory   tests   on   its   liver   showed   that   it   was   higher   in Vitamin    A    content    than    any    other    fish    tested.    After    this discovery,    it    became    the    subject    of    a    much    larger-scale fishery    which    developed    as    a    result    of    the    high    prices obtainable    for    the    fish    and    its    liver.    It    became    the    main source   of   supply   for   vitamin   A   in   the   United   States   during World    War    II,    but    was    overexploited,    populations    were reduced, and the numbers of fish caught dwindled. The   IUCN   has   classified   this   species   as   "Vulnerable"   in   its 'Red   List   of   Threatened   Species’   meaning   that   it   is   at   a   high risk     of     becoming     endangered.     In     2010;     Greenpeace International added the school shark to its seafood red list. The   IFCA   and   AT   have   placed   a   national   restriction   on   landing Tope    from    a    boat,    which    includes    kayaks    and    are    legally required   to   be   released   as   soon   as   possible   after   capture.as ordained by the Tope (Prohibition of Fishing Order) 2008. The   Tope's   teeth   are   designed   for   cutting   not   crushing   so   if caught accidentally watch your fingers when disgorging! Hand-haul   on   board   by   holding   the   pectoral   fin   and   tail   or   use a   large   net.   Do   not   gaff   and   remember   that   the   male   has   two claspers   it   uses   in   copulation,   so   care   should   be   taken   to avoid   grasping   them   as   they   can   inflict   a   painful   wound.   You must return them to the sea unharmed.

SEASONS

The   Tope   is   found   in   shallow   water,   small   specimens   near   to the   shore   and   others   down   to   200   m.   Usually   it   lives   in   small schools,   close   to   the   bottom,   although   when   feeding   it   is   in mid-water. It is seasonally migratory in northern waters.

The   Ling   is   the   largest   member   of   the   Cod   family   built   for   strength rather than speed and resembling a cross between a Cod and Conger. Preferring   very   deep   water,   Ling   are   abundant   at   3   to   400m   showing regularly   on   deep   water   reefs   and   wrecks   at   30m.Abootom   feeder   all the year round, winter produces the heaviest fish. It   breeds   between   March   and   July,   most   of   the   spawning   grounds   to the   north   of   the   British   Isles   in   the   areas   of   South   Iceland,   West   Faroes and South of Ireland, at depths of 1,000 metres. This   species   is   the   most   prolific   of   all   fish   for   spawning,   with   the female   producing   20-60   million   pelagic   eggs   each   season   which   float   at the surface whilst they develop. The   skin   is   very   unusual   in   that   it   resembles   rubber   sheeting,   so   a   very sharp knife is required for gutting. Hooks   should   be   needle   sharp   to   assist   penetration   as   striking   in   deep water   tends   to   lose   a   lot   of   its   force.   Once   hooked   this   fish   can   take considerable   effort   to   bring   to   the   surface   so   use   a   butt   pad   to   get   it away from the wreck or bottom quickly and never give any line. The   change   in   water   pressure   expands   the   swim   bladder   when   bringing to   the   surface,   usually   all   but   killing   the   fish   so   do   this   slowly   to   allow it to adjust to the change if you do not want to keep it. Ling   are   mainly   caught   commercially   on   long   lines   as   apposed   to   nets. It   is   a   valuable   commercial   fish   especially   in   Europe   especially   as   they have   a   long   large   liver,   which   is   high   in   oil   content,   regarded   as   a speciality in Scotland. It   can   be   considered   interchangeable   with   Cod   in   either   its   fresh, salted,   or   dried   forms.   The   salted   roe   is   considered   a   delicacy   in   Spain and   is   known   as   'Huevas   de   maruca'   and   when   Ling   is   treated   with   lye, is   made   into   'Lutefisk   '   a   traditional   dish   of   some   Nordic   countries. Some   of   the   catch   is   also   marketed   fresh,   a   little   is   smoked,   but   most is    salted    and    dried    for    local    consumption    and    export    to    southern Europe.

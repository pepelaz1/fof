LING (Molva molva

)

Preferring   very   deep   water,   Ling   are   abundant   at   3   -   400   m but   it   shows   on   deep   water   reefs   and   wrecks   at   30   m-plus.   A bottom    feeder    all    the    year    round,    winter    produces    the heaviest fish.
Additional    help    to    identify    this    species    can    be    found    in Google images using the following link;   www.google.com
Dull   browny-green   and   mottled   on   the   back,   lighter   below;   a dark   spot   on   the   hind   edge   of   the   first   dorsal   fin,   similar   but fainter   spots   on   the   second   dorsal   fins;   the   edges   of   these fins light.
Attains   a   length   of   2   m   and   a   weight   of   35   kg   usually   1   -   1.5 m in inshore waters.
The    members    of    the    cod    family    (Gadidae)    are,    with    one exception,    all    marine    fishes.    Most    are    found    in    the    cool temperate   waters   of   the   northern   hemisphere   generally   on   or close to the continental shelf. Few   are   found   in   the   deep   sea   and   then   only   near   the   surface or   in   the   southern   hemisphere.   Their   fins   lack   spines,   and they   usually   have   2   or   3   dorsal   and   1   or   2   anal   fins.   A   barbel on   the   chin   is   normal,   and   they   all   have   small   to   minute scales. Approximately    100    species    are    known    world-wide,    but    no more   than   30   live   in   European   waters   one   of   which   is   the Ling.
The   Ling   is   the   largest   member   of   the   Cod   family   built   for strength   rather   than   speed   and   resembling   a   cross   between   a Cod and Conger. Preferring   very   deep   water,   Ling   are   abundant   at   3   to   400m showing    regularly    on    deep    water    reefs    and    wrecks    at 30m.Abootom   feeder   all   the   year   round,   winter   produces   the heaviest fish. It    breeds    between    March    and    July,    most    of    the    spawning grounds   to   the   north   of   the   British   Isles   in   the   areas   of   South Iceland,   West   Faroes   and   South   of   Ireland,   at   depths   of   1,000 metres. This   species   is   the   most   prolific   of   all   fish   for   spawning,   with the   female   producing   20-60   million   pelagic   eggs   each   season which float at the surface whilst they develop. The   skin   is   very   unusual   in   that   it   resembles   rubber   sheeting, so a very sharp knife is required for gutting. Ling   are   mainly   caught   commercially   on   long   lines   as   apposed to   nets.   It   is   a   valuable   commercial   fish   especially   in   Europe especially   as   they   have   a   long   large   liver,   which   is   high   in   oil content, regarded as a speciality in Scotland. It   can   be   considered   interchangeable   with   Cod   in   either   its fresh,   salted,   or   dried   forms.   The   salted   roe   is   considered   a delicacy   in   Spain   and   is   known   as   'Huevas   de   maruca'   and when    Ling    is    treated    with    lye,    is    made    into    'Lutefisk    '    a traditional   dish   of   some   Nordic   countries.   Some   of   the   catch is   also   marketed   fresh,   a   little   is   smoked,   but   most   is   salted and    dried    for    local    consumption    and    export    to    southern Europe.

Hooks   should   be   needle   sharp   to   assist   penetration   as   striking in   deep   water   tends   to   lose   a   lot   of   its   force.   Once   hooked this   fish   can   take   considerable   effort   to   bring   to   the   surface so   use   a   butt   pad   to   get   it   away   from   the   wreck   or   bottom quickly - and never give any line. The   change   in   water   pressure   expands   the   swim   bladder   when bringing   to   the   surface,   usually   all   but   killing   the   fish   -   so   do this slowly to allow it to adjust to the change.

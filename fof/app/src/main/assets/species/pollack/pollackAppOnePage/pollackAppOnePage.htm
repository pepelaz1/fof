The Pollack conservation status is  ‘Least Concern.’

POLLACK (

Pollachius pollachius)

The    more    mature    Pollack    are    demersal    around    deepwater ledges,    rocks    and    wrecks,    smaller    species    tend    to    be    less restrictive   and   occur   over   sandy   shores   and   even   in   estuaries. Its depth range is from the surface to 200 m.
Additional    help    to    identify    this    species    can    be    found    in Google images using the following link;   www.google.com
Dark   brownish-green   on   the   back,   shading   to   yellowish-green on the sides, white underneath. The lateral line is dark.
The    members    of    the    cod    family    (Gadidae)    are,    with    one exception,    all    marine    fishes.    Most    are    found    in    the    cool temperate   waters   of   the   northern   hemisphere   generally   on   or close to the continental shelf. Few   are   found   in   the   deep   sea   and   then   only   near   the   surface or   in   the   southern   hemisphere.   Their   fins   lack   spines,   and they   usually   have   2   or   3   dorsal   and   1   or   2   anal   fins.   A   barbel on   the   chin   is   normal,   and   they   all   have   small   to   minute scales. Approximately    100    species    are    known    world-wide,    but    no more    than30    live    in    European    waters    one    of    which    is    the Pollack.
Pollack is an important species in recreational fisheries. The   Pollack   is   a   popular   member   of   the   Cod   family   widely distributed   in   European   waters;   good   for   a   tough   fight   when hooked. Like    Cod    they    are    plagued    by    parasites    and    use    grunting noises    by    vibrating    the    muscles    of    its    swim    bladder    when mating or showing aggression. This    fish    resembles    the    Whiting    and    is    also    frequently confused   with   young   Saithe   though   Pollack   is   brass   coloured and much coarser. Pollack   are   benthic,   that   is,   they   live   near   the   sea   floor   and considered to be relatively sedentary. Pollack     are     fast-growing     and     relatively     short-lived.     The maximum reported age is 15 years. The   female   spawns   between   January   to   April   in   deep   water   at 100   -   200   m,   although   their   spawning   grounds   are   poorly known;   a   study   of   a   fjord   population   in   Norway   suggested local   spawning.   The   eggs   and   larvae   are   pelagic,   living   at   or near   the   surface,   drifting   inshore   to   hatch   and   mature.   Young in their first year are particularly common in shallow water.

Inshore   drift   feathering,   trolling   or   spinning   at   dawn   and   dusk or   on   dull   summer   days   produces   the   juvenile   fish   that   live   in shallow waters over rocks. Large   solitary   Pollack   in   deep   water   are   taken   by   using   a flying   collar   rig.   During   tide   runs   this   method   will   yield   good- sized    specimens,    especially    in    mid-water    above    wrecks    or reefs. Keep   the   bait   on   the   move   by   lowering   to   the   bottom   and slowly   retrieving.   Lengthen   the   trace   as   the   tide   slackens   so that there is plenty of movement of the bait. On   bringing   the   Pollack   up   from   the   depths   the   swim   bladder expands,   so   do   this   slowly   to   avoid   impairing   its   resistance or, indeed, killing the fish.
Attains   a   length   of   130   cm   and   a   weight   in   excess   of   11   kg. Usually around 50 cm and 4 kg.

The   Skate   family,   Rajidae,   is   related   to   the   Shark   and   includes   the Arctic,   Black   and   Long   nosed,   all   of   which   are   rare   in   British   waters. Some   20   species   of   Skate   /   Rays   are   known   in   the   waters   of   the   north east   Atlantic   their   identification   frequently   presents   many   problems, which    are    often    compounded    by    differences    in    body    shape    of development of skin spines within the same species. The   Common   skate   is   the   largest   skate   in   the   world   attaining   a   length of   up   to   2.85   m   (9.4   ft).   Historically,   it   was   one   of   the   most   abundant skates   in   the   northeast   Atlantic   Ocean   and   the   Mediterranean   Sea. Despite   its   name,   today   it   appears   to   be   absent   from   much   of   this range.    Where    previously    abundant,    fisheries    directly    targeted    this skate   and   elsewhere   it   was   caught   incidentally   as   bycatch.   The   species was   listed   to   'Critically   Endangered'   on   the   IUCN   Red   List   in   2006   and it is protected within the EU. Skate   is   well   adapted   to   live   on   the   sea   bed   and   from   birth   is   flattened like   a   Ray.   The   Common   Skate   matures   at   around   eleven   years   of   age and   can   live   to   over   50.   Its   age   can   be   determined   by   counting   the rings of its vertebrae, as with a tree. Skate   have   well   developed   nostrils   and   depend   on   their   sense   of   smell to   detect   food.   They   also   possess   organs   that   detect   electrical   impulses of approaching prey and possibly permit species recognition. Male   Skates   have   pelvic   claspers   by   which   spermatozoa   are   introduced into   the   genital   opening   of   the   female   fertilization   is   thus   internal. Female    Skate    are    oviparous,    shedding    eggs    from    body    before hatching,   laying   the   eggs   on   the   sea   bed   between   February   and   August in   large,   14-25   cm,   dark,   oblong   leathery   cases   with   a   projection   at each   corner   known   as   skate   barrows,   sailor's   purses,   mermaid's   purses or   pin   boxes.   The   newly   hatched   young   measures   about   21   cm   in length.   Males   become   sexually   mature   at   1.5   m,   females   when   slightly larger. Jumbo   skate   are   only   found   around   the   western   Irish   and   Scottish coasts    where    localised    deep-water    areas    are    known    to    produce specimen catches. The   easiest   way   to   distinguish   these   Skate   from   a   Ray   is   to   place   a straight   edge   from   nose   to   wing   tip   and   if   any   part   of   the   body   edge crosses that line, then the fish is a Ray.

SKATE  (Raja or Dipturus batis)

aka: Common, Flapper, Blue or Grey Skate

Additional   help   to   identify   this   species   can   be   found   in   Google images at the following location;   http://www.google.co.uk
Dark   olive   brown   to   grey   above   with   light   brown   blotches   with darker   spots.   Ventrally   blue-grey   or   ash-grey,   with   lines   of black   pores   conspicuous   after   the   mucus   has   been   removed. Skate   that   are   semi   -benthic   frequently   have   dark   or   dusky undersides.

SEASONS

Skate   is   well   adapted   to   life   on   the   sea   bed   and   from   birth   is flattened    like    a    Ray.    Some    however    are    semi-benthic    only foraging off the bottom and feeding on mid-water fishes.
The   Skate   is   the   largest   and   heaviest   European   ray.   Females attain   a   length   of   2.85   m   and   a   width   of   2   m.   males   attain   a length of 2.05 m. a weight of 113 kg has been reported.

The   Inshore   Fishery   and   Conservation   Authorities   (IFCA)   have considerable   responsibility   for   managing   fish   stocks   out   to   6 miles   from   the   shore.   While   the   IFCA's   still   have   to   adhere   to the   rules   laid   down   by   the   European   Union   Common   Fisheries Policy   they   are   able,   through   by-laws,   to   set   minimum   landing sizes   that   exceed   the   European   Union's   and   apply   to   all   fish retained including those caught by recreational anglers.
* Restrictions in some IFCA regions. a) Any Skate or Ray that measures less than 40 cm between the extreme tips of the wings; or b) Any wing which measures less than 19 or 20 cms in its maximum dimension and which is detached from the body of a skate or ray. Always check with local Authorities on latest landing rights.

FACTS

The    Skate    family,    Rajidae,    is    related    to    the    Shark    and includes   the   Arctic,   Black   and   Long   nosed,   all   of   which   are rare   in   British   waters.   Some   20   species   of   Skate   /   Rays   are known    in    the    waters    of    the    north    east    Atlantic    their identification   frequently   presents   many   problems,   which   are often     compounded     by     differences     in     body     shape     of development of skin spines within the same species. The   Common   skate   is   the   largest   skate   in   the   world   attaining a   length   of   up   to   2.85   m   (9.4   ft).   Historically,   it   was   one   of the   most   abundant   skates   in   the   northeast   Atlantic   Ocean   and the   Mediterranean   Sea.   Despite   its   name,   today   it   appears   to be    absent    from    much    of    this    range.    Where    previously abundant,   fisheries   directly   targeted   this   skate   and   elsewhere it   was   caught   incidentally   as   bycatch.   The   species   was   listed to   'Critically   Endangered',   extremely   high   risk   of   becoming extinct   in   the   wild,   on   the   IUCN   Red   List   in   2006   and   it   is protected within the EU. Skate   is   well   adapted   to   live   on   the   sea   bed   and   from   birth   is flattened   like   a   Ray.   The   Common   Skate   matures   at   around eleven   years   of   age   and   can   live   to   over   50.   Its   age   can   be determined   by   counting   the   rings   of   its   vertebrae,   as   with   a tree. Skate   have   well   developed   nostrils   and   depend   on   their   sense of   smell   to   detect   food.   They   also   possess   organs   that   detect electrical   impulses   of   approaching   prey   and   possibly   permit species recognition. Male   Skates   have   pelvic   claspers   by   which   spermatozoa   are introduced   into   the   genital   opening   of   the   female   fertilization is   thus   internal.   Female   Skate   are   oviparous,   shedding   eggs from   body   before   hatching,   laying   the   eggs   on   the   sea   bed between    February    and    August    in    large,    14-25    cm,    dark, oblong   leathery   cases   with   a   projection   at   each   corner   known as   skate   barrows,   sailor's   purses,   mermaid's   purses   or   pin boxes.   The   newly   hatched   young   measures   about   21   cm   in length.    Males    become    sexually    mature    at    1.5    m,    females when slightly larger. Jumbo   skate   are   only   found   around   the   western   Irish   and Scottish   coasts   where   localised   deep-water   areas   are   known to produce specimen catches. The   easiest   way   to   distinguish   these   Skate   from   a   Ray   is   to place   a   straight   edge   from   nose   to   wing   tip   and   if   any   part   of the body edge crosses that line, then the fish is a Ray.

This   species   is   classified   as   ‘   Critically   Endangered’   and   although there   are   no   landing   size   restrictions   published   by   the   Anglers Trust the IFCA does. Fish   for   Skate   as   Thornback   -   leaving   the   bait   on   the   sea   bed   as   it likes   to   lie   on   it   before   taking.   Wait   for   a   continuous   bend   of   the rod   tip   before   striking   then   sustained   pressure   will   pull   the   fish   off the bottom. To   land   use   two   gaffs,   one   in   each   wing   behind   the   ridge   of cartilage   but   away   from   the   main   body   where   the   vital   organs   are located.   Although   not   legally   protected,   this   species   is   becoming rare, so please return your catch without damage.

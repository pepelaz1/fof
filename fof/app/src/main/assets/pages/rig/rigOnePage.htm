French Boom: With Muppet / Lure
A   type   of   rig   using   a   boom   to   keep   the   hook   trace   well   away from   the   main   line   as   the   tackle   descends   to   the   seabed   or when drift fishing. The   boom   is   now   usually   made   of   plastic   and   fixed   to   the main    line    by    threading.    It    can    be    from    6”    or    more dependant on the species being targeted.

Boom

Easy   and   quick   to   arrange   as   main   line   is   wrapped   around   the boom. Used   when   fishing   very   long   traces,   up   to   20ft   at   times   when wreck fishing. A   wire   trace   with   a   Muppet   or   mono   with   a   lure   are   just   two combinations that can be used.

Feathers

Feathers, Baited
A    fishing    lure    which    is    a    type    of    artificial    fishing    bait designed     to     attract     a     fish's     attention.     The     lure     uses movement,    vibration,    flash    and    colour    to    bait    fish.    Many 'feathers'   are   equipped   with   one   or   more   hooks   of   various sizes that are used to catch fish when they strike the lure. Feathers   are   usually   bought   already   made   up   for   you   and come   in   many   hook   sizes   and   trace   line   strengths.   These range   from   micro   hook   for   the   smaller   species   to   6/0+   which are   used,   sometimes   in   combination   with   pirks   for   fishing   on wrecks
These   booms   are   now   mainly   superseded   by   those   made   of plastic and are much stronger. This      rig   shows   a   wire   trace   that   would   be   used   for   catching Cod, Haddock, Ling, Pollack and Saithe.

Feathers, With Pirk
This   two   hook   rig   can   be   used   for   a   variety   of   species   that inhabit the sea bed (Demersal). By   using   a   low   strength   mono   line   to   attach   the   weight, called   a   ‘rotten   bottom’   should   the   weight   get   trapped   in rocks   or   some   other   obstruction   you   can   pull   the   line   back   in and only lose the weight.

Float

An   item   of   angling   equipment   that   is   attached   to   the   fishing line which serves several purposes. Firstly,   it   can   suspend   the   bait   at   a   predetermined   depth; secondly,   due   to   its   buoyancy,   it   can   carry   the   baited   hook to    otherwise    inaccessible    areas    of    water    by    allowing    the float   to   drift   in   the   prevailing   current;   and   thirdly,   a   float also serves as a visual bite indicator. These   rigs   are   rarely   used   in   boat   fishing   but   a   great   way for   a   change   to   try   to   capture   those   fishes   (Pelagic)   that inhabit     the     surface     or     near     surface     waters     such     as            Mackerel , Sharks, Spurdog and Garfish. Float   rigs   are   often   used   when   fishing   from   rocks   and   other shore    line    sites.    The    type    of    float    to    be    used    varies dependant    of    species    being    targeted,    weather    and    sea conditions as well as fishing location.

Ledger

Ledger   rigs   are   one   of   the   two   main   methods   of   getting   your bait to the sea bed the other being Paternoster. This   terminal   rig   has   the   weight   attached   to   the   main   line using   a   swivel   or   boom   allowing   the   main   line   to   be   extended and    not    move    the    weight.    This    allows    the    trace    line    and hooks   to   be   placed   on   seabed   and   a   direct   contact   between hook and rod to feel/see bites. Used when at anchor. The    weight    can    be    conical    in    shape    so    that    it    remains stationary   on   the   seabed   or   it   can   be   pear   shaped   enabling   it to roll over the  sea bed when searching for the fishes. The   end   trace   can   be   wire   or   mono   of   different   strengths   and enhanced with spoons, beads or just the hook.

Trolling

Spinning

This   is   when   a   lure   is   drawn   through   the   water   at   either   a variable   or   constant   speed.   Lures   can   be   fish   shaped,   flat,   or a   circular   blade   that   can   revolve.   The   more   narrow   and   oval shaped   this   blade   is,   the   tighter   it   revolves   around   the   lures body    and    at    a    greater    speed.    Widen    and    circularize    the shape,   and   the   blade   has   a   more   eccentric   movement   and revolves    slower,    but    gives    off    far    greater    impulses    to    be picked up by the fish.
Drawing   a   lure   across   an   area   likely   to   have   fish   at   a   slightly higher speed than the tidal flow.

Sharking

A    balloon    is    used    to        act    as    a    float,    with    ‘rubby    dubby’ discharged over the side. NB.     (Total ban for landing Porbeagles)
1. Locate wreck and drop marker buoy as close as you can to use as a reference point.

Wrecking

2. Starting near the marker buoy circle round until you have found the wreck and drop second buoy into the wreck then retrieve marker. Use a wrecking anchor which is usually made out of re- enforcing bar steel rods that will bend out when placed under strain.
3.   Steam   uptide   to   a   position   so   that   on   drifting   back   the   boat   will go over the wreck. Lower   rigs   to   the   seabed   and   as   wreck   is   reached   wind   up   to   clear dropping back down again . Repeat steaming back uptide and drifting back.
The   best   type   of   rig   to   to   use   will   depend   on   where   the   species you want to catch are normally found for that time of year. When   you   have   the   possibility   in   finding   different   species   in   a variety   of   waters   that   could   include   near   the   shore   line,   shallow waters,   rocks,   reefs,   sandbanks,   deep   mid   water   or   bottom   seas and wrecks a good knowledge of which rig to use is invaluable. Fish   are   mostly   migratory   and   a   favourite   location   can   provide good   catches   one   day   but   nothing   the   next   so   it   may   not   be   the type of rig or choice of bait that is at fault if not productive. There   are   so   many   more   rigs   than   those   shown   here.   Having knowledge    of    a    few    will    help    start    improving    your    chances. Checking   out   the   latest   angling   publications   will   provide   many excellent alternatives that use the latest equipment. Here   is   a   representation   of   well   known   methods   of   setting   up your   tackle   to   offer   the   bait   in   different   ways   in   an   endeavour to tempt your targeted species. It   is   well   known   and   often   happens   that   ,   even   when   targeting only    one    type    of    species,    the    recommended    rig    can    catch something totally different.

TACKLE: LINES
Early   fishing   lines   were   braided   natural   materials   like   silk   and therefore   quite   expensive,   modern   lines   are   made   from   synthetic materials like Nylon, Dacron, Dyneema, and Fluorocarbon. According   to   Wickipedia   it   was   written   in   1667   by   Samuel   Pepys that   the   fishing   lines   in   his   time   were   made   from   catgut.   Later, silk fishing lines were used around 1724. A   quick   search   on   Google   for   sea   fishing   lines   provides   a   choice of   over   362   manufacturers   so   heavens   know   how   many   different lines   are   available   for   sea   anglers.   Well   known   in   the   UK   are,   in no    particular    order,    Shimano,    Daiwa,    Berkley,    Penn,    Korda, Shakespeare etc. For   sure   there   will   not   be   one   line   that   suits   all   occasions.   Lines are    produced    with    different    characteristics    such    as    strength, flotation,   elasticity,   thickness,   colour,   length   and   material;   some costing   considerably   more   than   others.   To   name   them   all   would not   help   to   choose   the   right   one   but   knowing   the   characteristics of   each   one   will   help.   With   the   wrong   type   of   line   your   success rate   will   be   disappointing   and   frustrating.   You   need   the   right   type of   line   for   the   species   you   are   targeting   to   have   the   best   chances for catch and very importantly landing. Lines,   for   sea   anglers,   will   mainly   be   of   a   higher   breaking   strain than   those   used   in   coarse   fishing   and   some   aspects   attributed   to coarse   fishing   lines   are   not   always   relevant   such   as,   Floating   and Sinking   etc.   Not   that   lines   usually   used   for   fresh   water   do   not have   a   place   when   float   fishing   for   Mackerel   or   Garfish   provide excellent sport on light rods. Importantly   for   all   fishing   line   types   is   its   breaking   strength,   this is   the   amount   of   force   the   line   can   hold   before   it   parts   expressed in   pounds.   So   a   10-lb   test   fishing   line   will   hold   10   pounds   before it breaks and 30-lb test holds 30 and so on. Once   decided   which   species   you   want   to   catch   it   is   recommended by   the   experts   to   consider   the   following   regarding   the   type   of line specifically needed to give you the optimum chance. • Abrasion:   If   legering   need   high   resistance   if   jigging   this is not a factor. • Breaking   strain:    Comparative   to   the   likely   weight   to   be cast, presence of snags and size of fish to be caught. • Colour:    Do   you   need   to   ensure   the   line   blends   in   with   the waters? • Hook    size:     The    thickness    of    the    line    needs    to    be considered   for   going   through   the   eye   or   for   whipping   onto the shank. • Thickness:    The   thinner   the   line   the   less   profile   shown   to fish    and    more    supple    movement    aping    natural    bait behaviour. • Stretch:   Need   to   absorb   sudden   runs   and   lunges   or   a need for sensitive response to bites. • Flexibility:    Will    the    amount    of    ‘stiffness’    in    the    line, which    increases    with    the    breaking    strain,    make    a difference in casting or presentation on the seabed. It   is   good   to   remember   that   knots   will   decrease   the   line   strength from   10   to   20%   dependent   on   the   knot   and   how   carefully   it   is tied   so   the   general   rule   is   the   least   number   of   knots   should   be used. When   deciding   what   strength   of   line   to   buy   /   use   remember   that even   brand   new   line   will   provide   less   than   80%   of   its   rated strength once you’ve tied a hook or lure to it. All   knots   should   be   made   wet   before   tightening   up.   Using   spit   in the usual method. Any   line   being   used   must   be   checked   regularly   for   kinks   and abrasion   as   these   will   also   weaken   the   line.   Strip   off   the   first   5 metres   of   line   regularly   and   discard   in   a   save   manner   to   protect the   wildlife   and   environment.   On   no   account   must   you   throw   it into   the   sea   as   discarded   monofilament   fishing   line   takes   up   to 600   years   to   decompose   and   frequently   gets   fish,   birds,   turtles etc tangled up. Monofilament (Mono) This    is    a    single    strand    (mono)    of    nylon    filament    which    is invariably   incorrectly   referred   to   for   all   lines   irrespective   of   their     properties.   All   nylon   lines   will   stretch.   the   amount   of   stretch   from varies line to line and can be up to 25%. Advantages 1 . Many    manufacturers    providing    numerous    lines    with different   strengths,   colours   and   other   attributes   to   chose from. 2 . Cost     varies     immensely     dependant     on     quality     and manufacturer which provides competition in pricing. 3 . Because   mono   will   stretch   providing   help   in   stopping   the line    from    snapping    during    fish    make    sudden    runs    or lunges. 4 . Easier to tie than other types of lines. 5 . Almost   transparent   which   helps   blend   in   and   not   spook the fish, especially when jigging. 6 . Abrasive resistant for legering. 7 . More   buoyant   than   other   times   of   line   which   can   be   an advantage for float fishing. 8 . Easy to cast, with good coil memory. Disadvantages 1 . Because   of   the   competitiveness   in   pricing   the   quality   of some lines may not be as good as you would like. 2 . The   amount   of   stretch   with   mono   may   be   a   disadvantage for casting and feeling bites. 3 . Exposure to sunlight (UV) impairs the lines strength. 4 . Buoyancy   is   high   which   affects   sinking   rate   and   lying   on the sea bed. 5 . Tangle   prone   if   poorly   cast   (birds   nests)   which   difficult   to unravel
Fluorocarbon Similar to nylon monofilament and recommended by the professionals as one of the best all round lines available. Advantages 1. It has good resistance to abrasion even better than mono when compared like for like in diameter. 2. Having the same refractive index as water it does not visible which when fishing in clear waters  is a big bonus. Even when targeting larger species and need higher line poundage strength the diameter of the line does not matter as it is still invisible. 3. Not as buoyant as mono so it sinks quicker. This helps reduce, in deep water, the amount of slack line that occurs before reaching bottom. Any increase in size of a bow between bait and rod means more friction on the line by the tide so you need more weight to hold bottom and there is less response to bites. 4. The line lays and stays on the sea bed. 5. Able to take more weight before stretching so any bites are detected quicker to allow better striking. 6. Not affected by sunlight Disadvantages 1 . You    have    to    be    careful    what    type    of    knot    you    use, recommendations   are   Palomar   or   Trilene   and   the   use   of   a lubricant (spit!) when tightening the knot. 2 . Tangle   prone   if   poorly   cast   (birds   nests),   more   so   than Mono, which difficult to unravel 3 . Not a lot more unless you are picky!!
Braided Made   from   Polyester   (Terylene)   which   is   finely   spun   and   braided together    and    marketed    under    various    names.    The    fibres    are either fused together or left loose. Fused   line   minimizes   instances   of   looping   around   the   top   rod guide,   bird’s   nesting   on   a   spool   or   twisting   up   in   general   because it is stiffer than non-fused line. The   loose   finish,   not   fused   together,   remaining   as   strong   as   fused braided   line   but   has   is   more   prone   to   getting   birds   nests   etc   all because it is softer and more sensitive. The   tighter   the   braid   the   stiffer   the   line   becomes.   The   degree   of tightness   is   compared   by   the   'picks   per   inch'   and   varies   from   12 to   40   the   higher   the   number   the   stiffer   the   line.   Due   to   the   line having   a   hollow   cross   section   the   diameter   /   breaking   strain ratios   can   vary   if   the   material   is   squashed   on   measuring   with   a micrometer    hence    some    manufactures    do    not    publish    such figures. Super   lines,   also   referred   to   as   Multifilament   line,   are   a   loose finished    braided    line    and    marketed    by    proclaiming    they    are: Super   Strong   /   Totally   Translucent   /   Ultra   fine   diameter   &   low stretch   /   Extreme   Durability   /   Easy   to   tie   /   Super   Strong   /   100% PE Dyneema. By   weight,   polyethylene   strands   is   reported   to   be   five   to   ten times sturdier than steel The   strength   to   diameter   ratio   for   all   braided   lines   is   incredible but   this   can   be   reduced   considerably   by   incorrect   tying   of   knots. The Grinner or Palomar knot is usually recommended. A    more    supple    and    less    likely    line    to    kink    than    mono,    the additional    friction    coming    off    the    spool    (giving    less    casting distances)   and   more   vulnerability   to   abrasion   together   with   cost limits the appeal to use braided line. Advantages 1 . Less likely to kink than mono 2 . Higher    breaking    strain    relative    to    line    diameter    than mono. 3 . Denser material provides quicker sinking 4 . No memory from spool giving longer casting 5 . No   stretching   more   accurate   casting   and   better   ‘feel’   for bites. Disadvantages 1 . Some   braids   are   not   translucent   so   need   to   join   to   a   trace line that is e.g.Flurocarbon. 2 . Less    abrasive    resistant    than    mono.    When    used    for legering check for wear . 3 . More expensive than mono  
Wire Wire   is   mainly   used   for   traces   especially   when   targeting   those fish   that   easily   damage   mono:   e.g.   Angler,   Conger,   Haddock, Ling,   Monkfish,   Skate,   Rays   etc.   It   is   also   popular   for   some   types of trolling where reaching deep depths is important. Wire comes in single strand and braided varieties. Single   strand   steel   wire   was   popular   some   time   ago   especially when   fishing   deep   waters   over   banks.   Stored   on   a   large   diameter open    spool,    like    a    giant    fly    fishing    reel    the    wire    gave    good response   to   bites   but   had   to   be   used   with   caution   as   if   it   was caught   up   the   tension   would   build   and   could   snap   causing   injury. If    the    wire    develops    a    kink,    then    the    line    was    weakened considerably and more likely to snap. Braided    multi    strand    steel    wire    lines    are    made    up    of    a combination   of   metal   alloys   coated   with   plastic.   One   type   is made    from    titanium    nickel    alloys    which    is    reported    to    have several very important features which include: • flexible for single or multi strand • elastic    so    can    stretch    up    to    10%    without    permanent damage to the line itself • lines can be tied like mono • abrasion resistant • corrosion resistant • almost entirely kink-proof.

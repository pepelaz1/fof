TACKLE:  LURES
What is a ‘Lure’? In   the   English   language   to   ‘lure’   is   to   tempt,   entice   or   allure   a   person   or   animal   to   do   something   often regarded as unwise especially by offering some form of reward. In   fishing   terms   this   means   using   something   usually   artificial   (the   lure)   that   when   presented   correctly   will attract a fish to consider it as a tasty meal (temptation) and get itself hooked (unwisely). There   are   numerous   types   of   lure   available   that   can   be   made   from   plastic,   wood,   feathers,   metal   or   rubber some   of   which   are   in   two   pieces   with   a   flexible   link   holding   them   together.   All   of   which   have   unique properties,   i.e.   colour,   shape,   number   of   hooks   (single   or   trebles)   etc   which   when   used   in   a   certain   way   in the right waters will help catch the chosen species. You   can   never   tell   what   other   species,   other   than   the   one   targeted,   may   be   tempted   by   any   of   these   types   of lures it will just be by chance and it does happen which comes as a nice surprise. These are some of the types of lure available;
1.  Plugs: aka Crankbait, Popper or Wobbler. Plugs   are   hard-bodied   plastic   fish-shaped   fishing   lures.   Most   normally   float   on   the   surface   so that   after   casting   they   can   be   retrieved   either   fast,   slowly   or   in   spurts.   Dependant   on   type   they react   in   different   ways   e.g.   the   design   of   Divers,   having   a   vane   in   the   nose,   as   the   name suggests, will descend to various levels. As   they   come   in   many   different   sizes,   shapes   and   colours   it   would   be   impractical   to   provide details of them all. Most, however, come under the following categories; • Plug: Short and deep-bodied shape • Minnow: Long and slender • Shallow-diver: Shallow diving capabilities • Deep-diver. Deep diving capabilities
2.  Feathers aka Daylights A    very    popular    lure    for    catching    pelagic    (living    at    or    near surface)   and   demersal   (living   close   to   or   on   sea   bed)   species. Originally   the   lure   was   made   from   feathers   but   now   dressed more   likely   with   plastic.   Coming   usually   prepared   in   strings   of three   or   more   hooks   they   work   on   the   supposed   principle   that when   jigging   the   ‘feather’   looks   like   a   distressed   fish   which   is then taken by a larger fish. Like   other   lures   they   come   in   many   different   sizes   selected dependant   on   the   target   e.g.   micro   (16),   Mackerel   (8)   to   Cod (6/0)    as    well    as    various    shapes    and    colours    it    would    be impractical to provide details of them all.
3.  Spoons / Blades A   very   popular   lure   for   catching   benthic   (living   on   sea   bed) species.   As   expected   they   are   shaped   like   a   ‘spoon’   made   in white   plastic   or   chrome   metal   with   the   hook   attached   close   by. On   movement   the   spoon   attracts   the   predatory   fish.   Blades   are connected   to   the   rig   using   a   clevice   (is   a   three-piece   fastener system). Like   other   lures   they   come   in   many   different   sizes   ,   shapes   and sizes   so   it   would   be   impractical   to   provide   details   of   them   all   but the   shape   remains   very   similar   and   colours   can   be   painted   in different   colours   or   to   represent   a   small   fish.   New   variations regularly come on the market include one that has a rattle inside the spoon.
4.  Spinners When    a    spinner    is    retrieved    it    may    spin    as    well    as    wobble dependant   on   the   design   and   it   is   this   which   will   attract   the predatory fish thinking that it is a fish in distress!! Techniques   to   retrieve   the   spinner   after   casting   varies   dependant on what depth of water needs covering. Reeling   in   fast   keeps   the   spinner   near   the   top,   slower   allowing   it to   sink   will   go   through   mid   water   or   leaving   to   the   last   minute near   the   seabed   with   the   risk   of   ‘got   bottom’.   A   combination   of both slow and fast is called ‘sink and draw’ doing exactly that. One   of   the   newer   designs   of   spinner   is   the   ‘Reflective   Wedge’ which is more aerodynamic and better for casting. Like    other    lures    Spinners    come    in    many    different    sizes    and colours some more realistic than others.
5.  Jelly This   is   a   generic   name   for   lures   made   of   soft   jelly   like   plastic   or rubber   and   come   all   sorts   of   shapes   and   sizes.   Popular   styles   are mimicking   eels,   squid   and   worms.   Old   favourites   are   the   Muppet (squid) and Red Gill (Sandeel).
6.  Pirk / Jiggers For   deep   water   jigging   over   wrecks   or   banks   a   Pirk,   (heavy   tube shaped   solid   metal   tube,   that   has   usually   a   treble   hook   one   end and a swivel the other) is often used. A   Jigger   is   referred   to   as   a   type   of   blade   shaped   Pirk   popular   in Norway which produces a fluttering action when jigged. Pirks   come   in   many   other   different   shapes,   sizes   and   weights, two others shown give examples.
7.  Jig Heads These   are   made   from   lead   and   can   be   shaped   and   coloured   to represent a fish’s head otherwise they are a plain ball weight. A   hook   is   partially   embedded   so   that   the   shank,   bend,   point   and barb   is   showing   out   of   a   lure.   A   ring   provided   at   the   top   of   the head is used for fastening to the line.

RODS The   earliest   rods   were   developed   using   natural   materials   such   as ash,    greenwood,    lancewood    and    cane    but    they    sometimes tended   to   split   or   snap   under   relatively   low   strains.   These   rods were    also    prone    to    deteriorate    with    age    and    if    not    stored correctly   tended   to   take   on   a   set   (permanent   bend   or   twist). Split cane however is still used to make specialists rods. Advances   in   the   development   of   composites   such   as   Glass   Fibre, Carbon   (graphite),   Boron   and   Kevlar   has   dramatically   changed the   structure   and   dynamics   of   fishing   rods.   The   manufacturing processes   have   been   refined   resulting   in   the   production   of   rods that   are   comparatively   inexpensive,   maintenance   free   and   hold high tensile strength against weight ratios. During   manufacture   finely   woven   carbon   fibres   make   up   a   thin cloth   that   is   impregnated   with   resins   or   other   setting   agents   and rolled   round   a   solid   stainless   steel   mandrel   shaped   to   the   taper required.   This   cloth   is   sometimes   backed   by   others   made   either from   or   a   mixture   of   fibre   glass,   boron   or   Kevlar   (dependant   on the   type   and   eventual   cost   of   the   rod   concerned).   the   covered mandrel   is   then   heated   in   a   kiln   to   allow   the   resins   etc   to   flow together    and    harden.    Sanding    and    spraying    with    lacquer completes   the   process   to   produce,   after   removing   the   mandrel, a hollow rod blank. Glass fibre The   host   of   the   man-made   materials   used,   originally   solid   in section,   ideal   for   spinning   rods   as   having   a   soft   action,   and   now mass   produced   usually   hollowed   for   lightness   but   consequently likely    to    lose    strength    and    fold    under    pressure.    Only    the cheapest   range   of   rods   are   made   from   glass   fibre.   The   weight   to tensile   strength   ratio   is   surpassed   by   the   glass/carbon   rods   by 100%   and   pure   carbon   by   400%.   Glass   fibre   is   used   extensively for making quiver and swing tips. Glass / Carbon A    mixture    of    glass    Here    and    carbon    during    manufacture produces    an    efficient    cost    effective    rod    suitable    for    most applications and ideal for beginners. Carbon fibre (Graphite) An   ideal   material   as   having   the   properties   to   produce   a   rod   that has   great   rigidity   yet   is   flexible.   Carbon   fibre   is   expensive   to produce.    Polyacrylonitrile    (another    man-made    compound)    is exposed    to    high    temperatures,    circa    30000    Celsius    in    a pressurizes   inert   atmosphere.   Consequently   the   more   graphite used    in    a    rods    manufacture    the    more    expensive    it    will    be. Carbon   is   highly   conductive   material   and   care   should   be   taken when fishing near overhead power cables. (See Pole Code). Boron, Kevlar (or Aramid) These   other   man-made   materials   are   used   in   varying   quantities during   rod   making   to   improve   the   final   composition.   Boron   for strength   and   to   give   a   softer   action,   (and   mainly   used   in   fly rods), Kevlar to strengthen against impact damage. ROD HANDLES Cork Many   coarse   fishing   rods,   particularly   match   rods,   are   still   made with   full   cork   handles.   Natural   cork   is   waterproof   hard   wearing and comfortable to grip. Duplon This   material   (a   soft   rubbery   composition)   is   water   resistant, cheaper and more resilient than cork.
Test Curves All   rods   are   subjected   to   testing   to   calculate   their   breaking strains.   Their   rating   is   reached   when   the   tip   of   the   rod   is   at   right angles   to   the   butt.   The   rod   is   then   classified   according   to   the force required to do this. The   classification   determines   the   maximum   strength   of   the   line suited   for   the   rod's   strength   and   is   arrived   at   by   multiplying   the force (to pull the rod tip down at ninety degrees) by 5. An   example   would   be   for   a   rod   requiring   2lbs   for   bending   can safely   carry   10Ibs   line.   This   ensures   that   the   line   breaks   before the   rod.   Some   rods   are   marked   with   the   test   curve   rating   given to   the   nearest   quarter   of   a   pound.   Carbon-fibre,   however,   has   a greater     tolerance     and     the     stated     test-curve     is     only     an approximate,   i.e.   a   carbon   rod   with   a   2lb   test   curve   is   suitable for lines from 81b to 121b breaking strain.
Retrieving fish It   is   a   common   fault   to   play   fish   with   the   reel   rather   than   with the   rod.   Let   the   rod   do   the   work   and   use   the   reel   for   storing, casting and retrieving line. Always   'pump'   a   fish   towards   you   by   raising   the   rod,   and   always keep   the   rod   slightly   bent,   never   allowing   slack   line   to   develop which   could   lead   to   the   hook   falling   out.   Reel   in   the   line   whilst lowering the rod. Repeat,   as   often   as   necessary,   giving   line   to   the   fish   when   it suddenly   runs   or   dives   by   slipping   the   clutch   using   the   star drag   on   Multiplier   reels   or   back-winding   if   using   a   Centre   Pin reel. Winding   line   onto   the   reel   without   'pumping'   will   cause   excessive stretching   and   weakening   of   the   line.   Also   being   wound   too tightly around the spool will reduce casting distances.
Holding Rod
Holding   a   Multiplier   it   is   usual   set   so   that   to   retrieve   the   fish   the reel   is   on   top   of   the   rod.   You   can   then   access   all   the   settings   for the clutch etc.
1. On feeling a bite, strike then tighten line
2. Lift up rod keeping line tight by reeling in at the same time.
3.   Lower   rod   winding   in   any   slack   line.   Repeat   process   until   you can safely land the fish.
4.   Pulling   to   the   side   unbalances   the   fish   making   it   swim   in   an arc and helps to tire the fish for eventual easy of landing.
5.   Pulling   upwards   makes   the   fish   pull   harder.   This   places   a greater   strain   on   your   rod   and   tackle   so   should   be   completed smoothly without jerking.
To   maximize   your   chances   of   landing   a   fish   mainly   depends   on keeping the line tight at all times. Any   slack   line   will   increase   the   chances   of   the   hook   coming   out, the   line   breaking   on   a   surge   of   a   powerful   run   by   the   fish   and tangling with underwater objects. The saying given by anglers to others is; TIGHT LINES
